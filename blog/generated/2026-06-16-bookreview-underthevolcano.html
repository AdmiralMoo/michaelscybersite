Book Review – Under The Volcano
https://www.michaelscybersite.net/blog/2026-06-16
My copy of the book–a wonderful vintage Penguin Classic print from 1963. I love the way this print felt to read; old Penguin Classics are really something!

Going into this book, I didn’t really know what to expect. I picked it up at a bookstore because it was cheap and it had the Penguin logo on it—I felt an implicit trust for a publishing label which hasn’t failed me yet on blind buys.

It once again didn’t let me down.

I read this shortly after Ultramarine, Lowry’s previous novel, after I found that book in the giveaway pile at my school’s library. I greatly enjoyed his prose and writing style; it feels like the best of both worlds between the restraint, strife and pacing of Ernest Hemingway and the vibrant, lush, lyrical prose of James Joyce. For this reason alone I would recommend this book.

Set in the Mexican town of Quauhnahuac beneath two colossal volcanoes, the book follows a man at the end of the line as his life unravels completely. Though set on a day of festivities, the book is rife with anxiety over looming, inevitable devastation in multiple registers.

Geoffrey is an alcoholic—he is torn between the drinks which keep him afoot and the possibility of a return to a normal life with his now ex-wife, Yvonne. His idealistic half-brother, Hugh, comes to visit and complicates Yvonne’s return. Surrounded by friends and those who love him, Geoffrey struggles with isolation and his own self-destruction from his warped, hopeless perspective.

As we learn more about Yvonne, Hugh and Geoffrey and they go about their day, a feeling of inevitability builds through foreshadowing and Geoffrey’s conflicted internal dialogue. The book, set in 1938, also touches on the looming threat of the Second World War, adding an interesting layer to what otherwise feels isolated and small compared to the rest of the world.

Lowry, in real life, was an alcoholic—in fact, it’s what killed him in 1957. You could feel that in Ultramarine—a semi-autobiographical novel which featured parts where characters would drown their sorrows in alcohol. This life experience shows clearly in this booze-soaked, fatalistic, almost regretful novel. In many ways, it feels autobiographical in a future sense as if Lowry knew how his path would end. It’s absolutely fascinating to read.

Overall, it’s clever, interesting, surreal, and tragic. I was hooked from the start, read through it quickly and found myself feeling a void when it was over: that’s how you know it was a good book.
