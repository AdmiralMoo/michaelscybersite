There Will Never Be Another MVP Baseball 2005
https://www.michaelscybersite.net/blog/2026-03-13
My copy of the game. Note the original EB Games price sticker in the top right corner.

Since the beginning of home consoles, there have been baseball videogames—Atari’s Home Run (1978) on the 2600 is generally accepted as the first computerized baseball game. Nowadays, The Show series put out by Sony’s Santa Monica studios is the go-to for baseball. Throughout the 1990’s and early 2000’s in an era of intense competition and technological evolution, it was EA Sports at the top of the game.

On February 22nd 2005, Electronic Arts Canada released the latest entry in their yearly baseball sports series—MVP Baseball 2005—on the PlayStation 2, Xbox, GameCube and Windows. On the surface, it’s just a yearly sports game release, but the world has never seen anything like it since, and will likely never see anything like it again.

This is a love letter to what is regarded as the greatest baseball videogame—and possibly the greatest sports game—of all time.

FIRST IMPRESSIONS

The game's main menu. This is an odd photo of a direct capture of my PlayStation 2.

Back in 2007, I got a PlayStation 2 for Christmas after my cousin brought his console over to our grandmother’s house. Not too long after, I went to the mall with my Dad and browsed the abundant discount racks of EB Games. I got a copy of MVP Baseball 2005 for just $2.99 CAD—around $5.00 calculated for inflation in 2026.

We took it home and were greeted by Manny Ramirez’s pre-swing mugshot on the disc as I stuck it in the console—the now-iconic randomized intro featuring various players and fans across all teams played, the EA Sports man told me “IT’S IN THE GAME”, and “We Got The Noise” by the Donots sent that electric, euphoric feeling shooting through my veins for the first time. I created a profile and before I knew it, I was Roy Halladay on the mound at SkyDome.

From the moment you put the disc in and the “PlayStation 2” logo vwoomps in and out of existence, you can feel the sheer amount of care and love put into this game. The menus are fully animated with slick transitions the likes of which I’ve never seen since. The soundtrack is straight banger after banger—the music contributes so much to the atmosphere of the game and hooks you in.

FEATURES & CONTENT

A screen grab of Safeco Field during the mid-inning cutscene in a ballgame. It includes animated scoreboards, real sponsors, home run fireworks and moving crowds unique to their stadium. So much detail! A pitcher warming in the bullpen is visible in the bottom-left corner.

It features a full digital representation of all 30 MLB teams along with their AAA, AA and A farm teams. There are unique and highly detailed stadiums for each MLB team along with a roster of minor league stadiums to keep it interesting—each one feels like a completely different ballpark with unique sound design and atmosphere. Even today in the year 2026, you still can’t play all levels of minor league in The Show—and these newer games are quite lacking in additional ballparks.

There are a handful of game modes in MVP: you can pick up and play, play a post-season bracket, set your own circumstance, play entire seasons as an owner or manager, or battle through pitching and batting minigames. In all of these modes, you earn “MVP Points” for certain plays and achievements such as a double play or a stolen base.

As you accrue these MVP points, you can unlock historic jerseys, stadiums and players.

My cousin—Expos fan—taught me that you can unlock the full Montreal Expos get-up and play as that team in the game—Olympic Stadium, home and away uniforms and even the coach are all up for grabs. This game respects the history of each club and features home, away, alternate and historic jerseys for each team along with the greats of the past. DiMaggio vs Halladay? Young vs Jeter? It’s all there!

Recent editions of MLB The Show have added more historic players, retro jerseys and the like, but it was lacking for a long time and it’s still—in some ways—not as expansive as what MVP Baseballhad to offer. It most certainly isn’t as straightforward, either—modern games are riddled with micro-transactions and internet-based challenges.

My MVP Point earnings after Halladay threw a perfect game

The in-game action is well-paced and a lot of fun. Duane Kuiper and Mike Krukow—announcers of the San Francisco Giants—recorded hundreds of lines and quips for every scenario in the game. When Fred Rogers broke the single season Runs record, Kruk exclaimed that it’d stood since 1984 and he never thought he’d live to see Billy Hamilton’s 198-run season broken.

The lines are dynamic and funny, but they also tell you how to play the game. Sometimes when you forget to swing at a 95mph fastball down the middle, Kuip will say “that pitch was fatter than a thanksgiving turkey, but the batter wasn’t hungry!” They’ll tell you a swing was early or late, or if the pitcher looks like he’s getting tired out there on the mound. It’s blended so subtly into the commentary that you don’t even notice they’re coaching you, and when you succeed consistently, you really feel like you’re basking in the glow of the ball game with you.

You get that feeling throughout the game, really: it glows with a love of baseball and the game. I get excited when I play a game ofMVP Baseball. Even twenty years later, it keeps me guessing and I find new things to love about it.

Sure, it’s a PlayStation 2 game so things look a little dated, but I think the graphics and textures have held up well for the time. Many players and managers have their real faces imposed on models, and the big guns all had their swings, stances and pitching motions motion captured for posterity.

The EA Hitter’s Eye system makes it really easy to analyze your swings and improve your playing—it’s a lot like the detailed StatCast analysis’ we get on modern day television!

GAMEPLAY MECHANICS

THE GRAND SALAMI!

One thing I miss the most about MVP Baseball 2005 when I play modern games are the pitching and batting mechanics. While fielding hasn’t changed much over the years, I liked how simple batting and pitching was.

PCI and Ambush Hitting in MLB The Show 25 is a lot of fun and adds an extra challenge, but it sometimes feels unnecessary and overcomplicated. No game has ever recreated the satisfying and perfectly balanced feeling of MVP 2005’s timing-based pitch meter system—even the PlayStation Portable version of the game!

The batting cursor system was simple but incredibly precise—you weren’t fighting the interface, you were just trying to time the pitch and square up the ball. The DualShock 2, in the nineteen years that I’ve been using the same two controllers, does not suffer stick drift like the DualSense does. This alone—although unrelated to the game itself—improves immersion and removes a distraction when pitching.

Once you’ve played the game for a while, as with modern baseball games, you’ll figure out how to fool batters with various arrangements of pitches. There’s a full-on strategic pitching simulation in there just like real life, and it gets exponentially harder on higher difficulties.

Roy Halladay and the Toronto Blue Jays celebrate a win wearing retro jerseys. A low quality direct capture of my PlayStation 2

You can catch overzealous baserunners out on pitchouts and prevent them from stealing if your catcher and reflexes are good enough. You can steal home, you can achieve the mythical triple play (Kuip and Kruk will be ecstatic about it) and you can even make over the wall catches in the right ballparks. Fielders—CPU-controlled and player-controlled—will make mistakes, fumbling balls in the outfield or throwing past home on a routine double play, just like the real-life New York Yankees.

On higher difficulties in MVP 2005, umpires will make incorrect calls—you’ll find yourself yelling at the television asking if the ump needs glasses just like a real ballgame. Pitches will be difficult to predict and you will get caught out in the batter’s box when facing the masters of the mound.

MVP Baseball 2005 was one of the games which took advantage of the DualShock 2’s innovative pressure-sensitive button system. Vladdy Sr. can mash home runs to left field by aiming left with the analog stick and giving the X button a solid press. B.J. Upton can back out of a nasty curve by lifting off the X button quick enough. Checked swings are a similar system to what we have in modern games, but the nuance provided by the pressure sensitivity makes it so much more intuitive.

Replays take advantage of pressure sensitivity, too. In the game’s fully-fledged and surprisingly modern replay system, you can move around the ballpark freely and focus on the ball or a player, then fast-forward and quick-reverse through the most recent play. The seek speed will respond to how firmly you press down on L1 and R1, making it a lot easier to stop at a precise moment of action.

All in all, it felt like MVP Baseball 2005 had the controls and mechanics nailed down. It feels very natural and logical, which I think made it a lot easier for anybody to pick up and play. When you’ve played for a while, the game has enough depth that it’ll just keep delivering and challenging the player—this has been instrumental to the game’s longevity.

OWNER MODE

Roy Halladay celebrates the Toronto Blue Jays 2005 World Series win at home at the Rogers Centre. A screen grab from emulated gameplay in PCSX2, showing how sharp and detailed the game really is.

My favourite part of MVP Baseball 2005 is “Owner Mode”; you play as not just the General Manager but the owner of a ball club, making all the financial decisions from stadium upgrades, concession prices, scouting, coaching salaries, player transactions and lineup adjustments. You can zoom all the way out and balance the books, then zoom all the way in and play as the team that you’ve assembled in the ballpark you’ve budgeted for.

My favourite thing to do is return to my long-running Owner Mode save—the current one I’ve had going since around 2019. My Toronto Blue Jays won the 2005 and 2006 World Series consecutively, and I played every single game across those two seasons. I pick it up every so often and play a game here or there—I’m sometime in April of 2007 at the time of writing.

No baseball game—or sports game, for that matter—has ever allowed as much minute control over your team as this one does through Owner Mode. It’s the most detailed, granular simulation featuring team chemistry, promotional days, gift shops, the colour of your basepaths or your choice of astroturf versus grass. In recent MLB The Show games we got a Stadium Builder on PlayStation 5 exclusively, but that feature was unseen for twenty years after MVP Baseballdisappeared.

THE FALL & THE LEGACY

My copy of *MLB The Show 25*—what an upgrade 20 years makes! And yet I still come back to MVP Baseball 2005...

It was all too good to last, wasn’t it? They released what is widely regarded as the greatest sports game of all time, and then—as EA does—they dropped the ball.

TakeTwo Interactive got the exclusive contract for baseball simulation games in 2006, and the leftover MVP code was relegated for two college baseball releases in 2006 and 2007 until it was shuttered entirely.

The community didn’t stop, though. MVP Baseballon PC is still played to this day online with a lively modding community working tirelessly to keep rosters and jerseys up-to-date.

When I mention the game in conversation, people who don’t even play games remember MVP Baseball 2005 from when they were kids—they or their siblings had a copy lying around and they played a couple of games. It’s always recollected rather fondly.

A message from the Owner Mode inbox showing Fred Rogers' current league-leading RBI count. A photo of the television

MLB The Show, for the longest time, just didn’t feel the same. The graphics in MLB 10 felt dry and sterile compared to the realistic but stylized look of MVP 2005. By the time MLB 2015 rolled around and it became a more feature-complete game, it had no competitors and microtransactions began to seep into the game’s soul. Ten years later, MLB 25 The Show, I would say, is a very enjoyable and complete experience—but it still feels a little lacking in some ways.

I know that this is because I grew up with MVP 2005 and I have so many fond memories with it. It’s been there with me through everything: when I got home from elementary school, when things got harder later on, recovering from injuries, breakups, and countless hours spent building a narrative in my head about those virtual players. I’ve got nothing but love and fondness for this game, and I have no choice but to compare everything with it.

I played around a hundred and fifty hours cumulatively between MLB 24 and MLB 25, and I’ve greatly enjoyed those games. I feel like they’re proper, full and fun baseball simulators and they scratch that itch and then some. I’ll always find myself coming back to MVP Baseball 2005, though. It’s just a part of me. Before the baked-in online play, before the online trading card market, before the microtransactions, before the paywalls and before everything had to be ultra-realistic, there were games which did the best they could within their limitations: they had soul.
