Book Review – Across The River and Into The Trees
https://www.michaelscybersite.net/blog/2026-06-08
The cover of the edition of The Ringworld Engineers I read. It was a library book.

I picked up this book because I’ve greatly enjoyed Hemingway; I’ve read The Sun Also Rises, The Old Man and The Sea, To Have and Have Not, A Farewell To Arms twice and A.E. Hotchner’s Papa Hemingway—a book documenting the last decade of his life from the perspective of a close friend. I’d say that Hemingway has quickly become one of my favourite authors; I love his prose and writing style, and I enjoy what his books make me feel.

This one is easy to pass by. Although it’s not long in page count, it’s slow, rambling and not much seems to happen. It’s a long buildup of emotion in the tension between a terrible loss and preemptive mourning for the inevitable. In the devastating wake of war, it’s bittersweet and sad in a similar way to the early chapters of A Farewell to Arms: the characters feel like they’re clinging to the edge of whatever they have left. The dialogue is hollow, almost haunted.

In an article published for Esquire, Hemingway once said “When people talk, listen completely”. That quote perfectly sums up how you should approach this book—or any book, for that matter. When someone is talking, wait for them to finish speaking. Let it come together and see what they were leading up to. I wasn’t expecting it, but I felt shattered at the end of this book as I have with some of his others.

I can see how so many people thought negatively of it; much of the criticism feels like they didn’t take time to just sit with it and enjoy it for what it is. This is very easily not a book for everyone because of its style and pacing. As with many of Hemingway’s best prose, much is implied. If you’re not open to feeling it, you’ll miss it—there’s almost no immediacy here, it’s all payoff once you understand everything and it concludes.

It was quiet, but it was damn good. It’s the story of wounded souls dealing with what’s passed and facing the inevitable. Two people who meet and enjoy a pocket of respite, but the timing is all mucked up.

I would recommend this book. It’s probably not for everyone—it’s probably not for most people, in fact—but I rather enjoyed it. You might like this one if you enjoy analyzing characters, melancholic descriptions of post-war Venice or themes of mortality and ageing.

Let it unravel: it all wraps together nicely, but this is a Hemingway novel—it dies in the rain.
