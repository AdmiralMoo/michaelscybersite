9 Innings, 12 Hot Dogs – A Night At The Ballpark
https://www.michaelscybersite.net/blog/2026-04-14
The first batter: Shohei Ohtani. Shot on Ultramax 400 with my Nikon FM

For a couple months now, I knew that I would be back in Toronto for a school trip; I was already planning on going to a Jays game since it’d been a long time after moving out east. What I didn’t know was that April 7th, 2026, marked the 50th anniversary of the Toronto Blue Jays’ inaugural season—and that there would be $0.77 hot dogs to celebrate.

ARRIVAL

The CN Tower from Bay (Nikon Z6)

With my scholarly obligations having concluded, I walked down Bay St with my camera bag slung over my shoulder. I stopped a couple times to shoot photos of buildings, but I kept a speedy pace so that I’d have time to get to my seats before the first pitch.

As I grew closer to the Rogers Centre, fellow Blue Jays fans congregated and formed a herd. Through the crowd poked jerseys from every era of the team—Barger, Clement, Guerrero, Pillar, Encarnacion, Hill, Halladay, Carter and even a Martinez were amongst the sea of blue and white. I blended in with my Bautista shirt.

The CN Tower loomed above on the cloudless afternoon as golden hour drew nigh. I lined up at Gate 5 and waited to be inspected.

The security lady tried to turn me away because of my camera, but I explained to her that one of them is literally a film camera, and the other was equipped with a vintage lens from the 1970s. She gave a funny look when she realized how old my gear was and waved me through.

Inside, a flurry of people scuttled clockwise and counterclockwise around the stadium’s ground level. Clerks selling 50/50 raffle tickets advertised a pot of over one million dollars as families impatiently waited to meet up with their loved ones.

I headed west towards the 34’s and made my rounds up the ramp to the 500 level, passing tired children, unenthusiastic parents, leisurely fans and dudes carrying their weight in hot dogs.

It was nice to be back at the Rogers Centre.

EARLY GOINGS

The umpires congregate before the game begins (Nikon Z6)

April 7th was round two of a three game series against the Los Angeles Dodgers—the first time these two teams met since the Jays lost in Game 7 of the 2025 World Series.

The air crackled with electricity as I sat down in row 24 at the very top of the stadium. Kevin Gausman took his warmup pitches on the mound. My favourite active pitcher was to face Yoshinobu Yamamoto—a battle of the titans.

The Blue Jays lineup looked a little different than it did at the beginning of the season; there were already quite a few players on the IL. 1. DH George Springer 2. CF Daulton Varsho 3. 1B Vladimir Guerrero Jr. 4. LF Jesus Sànchez 5. 3B Kazuma Okamoto 6. 2B Ernie Clement 7. RF Nathan Lukes 8. SS Andres Giménez 9. C Brandon Valenzuela

Behind me, there was nothing but concrete—I was at the top of the world figuratively and literally. The newly renovated scoreboard shone brightly against the hotel in the outfield as special 50th anniversary graphics played. On the dugout, all of the teams’ logos were collaged together like stickers. Domer, Ace and BJ Birdy danced around the stadium on camera intermittently.

The lights came down after the national anthems were sung, and when they came back up, the Blue Jays sauntered onto the field to a roaring applause, which was quickly silenced and turned to sharp boo’s as Shohei Ohtani stepped into the batter’s box.

I can’t hate the guy—he seems like one of the friendliest dudes in baseball—but he did snub us when he went to the Dodgers, and he is one of the best players in the league across enemy lines. The booing is understandable.

Before the first pitch (Nikon Z6)

Gausman threw the first pitch, and the game was underway. The crowd ooh’d, aah’d, cheered and cried for each call. Live baseball is enthralling and alive. The immense stadium and 40,000 people inside it were as one, cheering for and against the little people scattered out on the green far below.

Freddie Freeman was the only Dodger to get cheers and applause—he’s Canadian. Kyle Tucker—the latest player who almost signed for the Jays but went to L.A.—had the loudest boo’s of the entire team.

The first two innings went by uneventfully, but it was exciting to watch the pitcher’s duel. I figured it wouldn’t be a very high-scoring game because of the matchup, but that’s okay. I love watching Gausman pitch on TV, and it was enthralling to see both him and Yamamoto on the mound for real.

The trouble didn’t start until the third inning.

LOONIE DOGS DAY

"Loonie Dogs Sold Here!" (Nikon Z6)

To commemorate the 50th anniversary of the Toronto Blue Jays’ first game at Exhibition Stadium, a special Loonie Dogs day was commissioned featuring $0.77 hot dogs—just $0.17 more than dogs were back on opening day that year.

I’d been up since 04:00 running on two medium coffees from Tim Hortons (very Canadian of me) and I was famished. I decided I’d take in some baseball then get my dogs in the third inning so that the lines wouldn’t be so long—the plan was solid, but the game began unraveling as I watched the television from the line.

It was easy to find a hot dog vendor. They were marked by special signs hung from the ceiling reading “LOONIE DOGS SOLD HERE!”

It took around five minutes to get through the line, and when I got to the counter, I asked the clerk for twelve hot dogs. He didn’t even bat an eye—I suppose the ridiculous orders had been coming in all evening.

My full box of dogs. Shot on iPhone 12 Mini

They ran out of bags and cartons, so I got twelve shining aluminum-wrapped dogs in a cardboard shipping box. I headed back up to the stands with my beer and sat back down. I laughed about the box of dogs with the people next to me.

Gausman let up two runs to L.A., but the game was back under control and I rejoiced as the Jays earned a run back in the fifth. I didn’t think we were going to win; we didn’t seem to be playing well enough to rally, especially after loading the bases and failing to capitalize on it.

I enjoyed the game and soaked it all in, slamming back dog after dog. They didn’t have any condiments on them, but they were really good just plain. They’re full-sized Schneiders hot dogs with what I believe were Wonder Bread buns.

LATE GAME

Many dogs eaten (Nikon Z6)

By around the sixth or seventh inning, the previous Loonie Dogs record had been broken. It was estimated there would be around 97,000 dogs consumed, but the ticker continued rising past 100,000 as the Jays played on.

It was exciting to see John Schneider argue the call and get ejected. That got everybody really riled up; it felt good to cheer for a manager who defends his team and makes himself heard. It felt good to cheer for him in person, not just on the television.

I signalled the beer guy far below me with two hot dogs. There was no way he’d come all the way to the top looking for customers. He laughed and said he enjoyed the antics when he made his way up—I was making signals like a ground marshaller at an airport.

A nice photo of a security guy (Nikon Z6)

When Hoffman came out, I booed him. Man, did that feel good! I don’t dislike a lot of players, but Jeff Hoffman is one of them. I’m sure he’s a decent guy and he’s had good games, but he was so painful to watch last season. Sometimes, it feels like I’m watching Lance Stroll if he took up baseball. That feeling was further reinforced when he made the error.

The ball was hit in the gap between first and second, and Hoffman ran over to cover while Vladdy retrieved it. Hoffman failed to catch the ball and it sailed past him, and when it was thrown back at him from the direction of home plate, he missed it again and a run scored. I can’t believe I watched an absurdly bad Hoffman play in person! It was a highlight of the game, to me.

I thought ABS was a cool addition to the game. It gave us something else to cheer about.

The Jays lost 4–1, but they’d come back the next day and win the last game in the series.

A photo showing the scoreboard at the end of the game: 102,202 dogs consumed (Nikon Z6)

The Loonie Dogs record was smashed out of the park—at the end of the day, 102,201 dogs were eaten. I had my first eleven in the stadium and I saved my twelfth for when I got back to the hotel—so started the legend of Michael’s “Pocket Glizzy”. It was all fantastic. Nobody believed it was my 12th until I showed them photos of my box of hot dogs.

I had a whale of a time despite us losing. The crowd was still in it until the end when it seemed like the Ern Dog was going to start a rally and we could maybe get it tied. When the game ended with Gimenez, there was a collective sigh and the good feelings continued as everyone filtered out, albeit a bit subdued.

At the end of the day, it was just a lot of fun to watch baseball live again. I haven’t been to a Jays game in a couple years and it felt both familiar and fresh at the same time. I’ve had so many great memories in that ballpark, and I’m glad to have made a couple more; I just can’t wait to go back! It’s totally different when you’re an adult, you’ve got a job and you can buy whatever crazy confections you want…
