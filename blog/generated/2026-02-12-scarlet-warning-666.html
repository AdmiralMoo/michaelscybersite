A Night At The Movies – Scarlet Warning 666

Last night at The Mayfair, my local movie theatre, I saw the third and final screening of Scarlet Warning 666. It was so good—so bad—that I had to see it a second time; the first was this past Sunday, the 8th of February.

It’s difficult to describe this film because it’s simultaneously so much and so little at the same time. I strongly believe that this is one of the best bad movies ever made—and I’ve watched a lot of good-bad movies. There’s a fine line between unwatchable and it’s-so-bad-its-kinda-charming: this movie dances perilously in the middle. After two one-hour and forty-seven minute screenings, I never felt like I didn’t want to be there.

The Plot (...I Think?)

Overall, it’s a demonic detective murder mystery. A private investigator, Bruce, is on the case after his twin sister goes missing. He believes that his twin brother Jackie has something to do with it because he’s allowed a demonic cult to occupy their father’s estate, radicalizing his sister into forbidden rituals involving the mysterious and aggressive Scarlet Guards.

Bruce runs around and interrogates various witnesses, including park-goers and the psychiatrist of his missing sister. As the investigation deepens and he gets closer to the answer, there are multiple attempts to take his life by the Scarlet Guards.

Eventually, he goes to confront his brother on the estate. It turns out there is hidden oil on the property and his brother along with one of his former adversaries from Pre-Med school, are going to cash in.

One of Jackie’s right-hand women—“The Bottle Blonde”—turns out to be Bruce’s twin sister, but both her and the Pre-Med adversary (Michael) is killed by a deadly eagle-shaped laser powered by the greed of capitalism.

It sounds relatively straight-forward, albeit still corny, when I write it that way, doesn’t it? It took me two viewings to fully understand that—if you miss any bit of dialogue or narration in this film, you’ll loose the plot.

But that’s where the fun begins. It gets real “ficky-fucky-monkey!”, as Bruce says.

Rockey's Way or the Highway

The whole “gimmick” of the film, so to speak, is that the egomaniac one-man-band director, Palmer L. Rockey, plays fifty-four (yes, 54!) roles throughout the film—seven of them on screen, forty-seven of them off-screen.

Rockey plays Bruce—the lead—and his twin-brother Jackie—the antagonist of the film. It’s a little confusing the first time around, but there are ample fourth wall breaks throughout the film where the director and omnipresent narrators—Rockey and Rockey High—explain what’s going on.

The story is that this movie was originally released in 1974, but Palmer Rockey kept splicing, reshooting and adding to the film throughout the 1980’s until his death in 1996. The film has been released under a couple of different names—the title card says “Love is Deep Inside”, the Letterboxd page is listed as “It All Happened In A Weekend”, and it’s referenced around the interwebs as “Scarlet Love” and “Rockey’s Style.”

Rockey’s Style…that brings us to the soundtrack of the same name. In addition—or rather, as a part of—Rockey’s 54 roles, he also wrote and sang every song in the movie. Admittedly, I really like the soundtrack of this movie and I’m going to grab a copy of it on CD. The bright yellow album filled with groovy lounge music is featured on vinyl throughout the course of the film.

The editing and writing is amateurish—it feels like someone got a hold of a camcorder and iMovie in 2007 and stitched something together…except this is the early 1970’s! For that alone the movie is very interesting to watch; old cars, roadways and infrastructure is all over the place captured in a way that doesn’t come across in professional productions.

They Don't Make Em' Like This Anymore (They Never Did)

It’s corny, funny and it doesn’t really make sense. It’s certainly more cohesive than The Rollerblade Seven, but not by much. If you love Scott Shaw’s Zen filmmaking, you’d love Scarlet Warning 666. I’d love to say that Palmer Rockey paved the way for Scott Shaw, but he really didn’t—this movie was barely screened in Texas and I doubt Shaw would’ve even known about it. This movie is so far up my alley there's no room for parking.

Just like horseshoe crabs and trilobites, this is a case of convergent evolution and it’s well worth the watch if you’re into this kind of thing. I would’ve gone back to see it again if there was another screening. This movie is a cult-classic to-be—it just hasn’t been around long enough for the cult to form.

Consider me Member 001!
