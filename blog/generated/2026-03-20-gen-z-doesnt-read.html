Loving Books in A Generation That Doesn’t Read
https://www.michaelscybersite.net/blog/2026-03-20
My annotated and bookmarked copy of Jane Austen's Persuasion. I strongly believe it's her best book.

The other day, I finished reading Giles Blunt’s John Cardinal series—six books and roughly 1,900 pages of vivid, gripping, unapologetically Canadian detective adventures in a fictionalized North Bay. In three and a half weeks, I read all six books, plus two extras in between. Those numbers are frankly absurd—I did this in the final stretch of my college program while working part-time.

Needless to say, I love reading a lot, and I’ve been reading for as long as I can remember. I don’t know many people who read, much less people who enjoy reading as much as I do.

Thinking back, it wasn’t always that straightforward either—I didn’t have a good time reading throughout elementary school, and in many ways, it’s really weird that I read as much as I do now.

While there are many reasons on all sides as to why reading is no longer prevalent, I don’t think that it was taught well or in an engaging way in school—even as someone who went into it loving to read already.

I thought it’d be interesting to go through my experience of reading at a young age, its struggle for relevance in Canadian classrooms and what the reading climate is like now as a College student in his twenties.

READING AS A CHILD

A fantastically vivid, dream-like book which feels very modern despite having been written over a hundred years ago: The Man Who Was Thursday

I’ve always had a healthy interest in reading thanks to my parents. Growing up, they were always reading something—my father enjoyed lots of Clive Cussler, Stephen King and various Science-Fiction while my mother loved detective and mystery novels. They read to me when I was a baby and fostered an interest in it, so by the time I got to school, I was already pretty proficient.

One of my favourite parts of the day in early grade school was the half-hour after lunch recess when everybody was made to be quiet and we’d read books. I went through all of the A–Z Mysteries and Animorphs series, and enjoyed lots of the non-fiction books, particularly about technology and computers.

I was dazzled by these portals into the world, painting pictures of ancient civilizations, cultures on the other side of the globe and magnificent technological advancements. I was bewildered to find out the marvelous Concorde had been retired for almost ten years—some of the books were hopelessly outdated.

I was excited to read whatever I could get my hands on, and I read a lot. There was always something from the public or school libraries for me to devour—and I loved every bit of it.

Things were pretty good until around third grade. This is when my relationship with reading became strained.

A SYSTEM WHICH DOES NOT ENCOURAGE READING

One of the happiest, most golden memories of Summer 2025: going on a long bike ride, reading The Pillow Book of Sei Shonagon and enjoying some fresh eggrolls

By this point, I’d finished all of the books which interested me and was left with nothing but “I-Spy” books, which I’d avoided like the plague. I enjoyed the photography and arrangements of objects, but I didn’t care for the spying, nor did I think it was a good use of Reading Time. The books in the classrooms didn’t really change from Grades 1 to 3.

I was at the top of the school board’s “reading levels” evaluation system—I was fully proficient in whatever criteria they were judging me by. That doesn’t mean they’ll give me more interesting books, though.

The school’s library had an “off-limits” section which was only for the Grade 7’s and 8’s. There were thicker, much more interesting books there.

I would ask to go to the bathroom during recess, then get older kids to reach up and grab books for me, or I’d just slip into the library and take them. On my way back outside, since we kept our backpacks in the halls, I would stow the books away. It’s quite nefarious and shocking, looking back—I really wasn’t much of a troublemaker, but I was full-on stealing!

I couldn’t return them, obviously, so I’d just leave them somewhere on a bench in a hallway and hope they’d get picked up and put back. Needless to say, I was eventually found out and I got in trouble a couple of times for doing this.

I still read books at home and from the public library, but I was assigned books at school. They were simple and boring, and I didn’t want anything to do with them. I wanted to read books like Blockade Billy, The Lightning Thief or even more Diary of a Wimpy Kid.

I remember one October day I had an assignment to read a book about a family who owned a mini-golf course. I don’t remember the name of this book or the plot—only that the cover was a grassy shade of green with an illustration of the family. One of the kids had a golden crown on his head.

I hated the book so much that I started to believe that I didn’t like reading. I’d read so many boring books I hated through school, and this was the last straw. I sat there at the kitchen table looking at the poor book like it was a pile of string beans and I just couldn’t get through it.

I didn’t read another book that year. I simply refused. That’s very sad, looking back.

PUSHBACK & RESIGNATION

In fourth Grade, I switched to a different school to start French Immersion. I convinced my parents to buy me the rest of the Percy Jackson series, and I read through those very quickly. I stole the Hunger Games trilogy from the school’s library, and the first three Heroes of Olympus books.

I enjoyed reading again, but I still struggled in school. We had to read the Geronimo Stilton books, which I thought were absolutely stupid. I didn’t like how words changed font, size and colour throughout the page, and I refused to read them in class and as homework. I told the teacher it was stupid, and I told that to the principal, too. Needless to say, I got in trouble for that.

There are moments I forget how needlessly stubborn I was.

It was more of the same throughout fifth and sixth grade as I progressed through The Maze Runner trilogy, The Lord of The Rings trilogy plus The Hobbit, and I got into more non-fiction such as Darwin’s The Origin of Species.

I went through a phase of being obsessed with the Galapagos islands.

I got into more Stephen King, with books such as Under the Dome and Duma Key—the latter of which was confiscated because it was “distracting” me, and then it was deemed “too mature”, despite being one of my parents’ books.

It felt like school didn’t really want me to read. There were kids kicking each-others nuts out at recess, and I just wanted to enjoy my book. I was slowly pushed away from reading as it wasn’t encouraged in the classroom and what I wanted to read wasn’t approved of. I hesitated and decided against bringing James Patterson’s Zoo to school, despite loving the book.

By the time seventh grade rolled around, I no longer brought books to school. Nobody else in class was reading, and assigned books were sparse. I’d had one in fourth grade, one in seventh, two in ninth and one in tenth and eleventh—that was it!

INTERESTING PRIORITIES

Absolute stupidity!

There was a sour attitude towards reading no matter what school I went to. Nobody wanted to read the assigned material, whether it be on their own or as a class. I dreaded listening to people struggle and slog through class-wide readings—nobody learned proper comprehension skills, and it was clear that by sixth grade, reading had calcified as an unpleasant chore for most.

Many kids would bring their iPods and DS’s to school to play games on during recess. They’d be confiscated (voluntarily) and given out during free times. It felt like it was easier to have an electronic device or music player than it was to bring a book to school. So I replaced the book with an MP3 player on the bus.

It felt like the teachers just wanted it to be over with whenever they had to assign readings or listen to the class read. Nobody was enjoying it, and nobody was excited for it. Probably the “best” book we collectively read in class was The Outsiders during seventh grade, and it took a lot of genuine enthusiasm from the teacher to get people hooked on it.

I think that if reading had been presented as something exciting, deeply engaging and captivating as that teacher presented The Outsiders, maybe a lot more people would be open to it.

At the same time, they introduced the initiative to have more computers in the classroom. The standard setup was to have two computers in the back of the class—Dell OptiPlex 740’s running Windows XP in my earlier years and OptiPlex 7010’s by the time I graduated, grandfathered on Windows 10. These two were more than enough for people to get some digital work done in a pinch, otherwise they’d go to the computer lab or library.

Someone thought it’d be a great idea to have Chromebooks and iPads. What they were supposed to do? I have no clue. I think it might have just been a half-baked sales pitch by tech companies to siphon schoolboard funding.

In fourth grade, my precious Reading Time was replaced with iPad Time. They’d dole out these iPad 2’s and we would have to play educational games for half an hour every day. I dreaded it, and I don’t think I ever learned anything. I hated the math games and how they were all time-based—I’m good at evaluating equations if I’ve got the formula and a bit of time, but adding pressure never helped. I hated the other games where you would have to match patterns and whatnot. Some people just played dress-up games which were apparently designed for learning.

Regardless of what you did on the iPad, most kids were hooked—those in charge had just replaced the boring chore of reading with backlit digital candy bars, and the audience was eating them up.

"Hey, put on some clothes!"

Chromebooks came into play at around the same time—cheap, manufactured e-Waste in a plastic shell running Android. There were never any classes on how to type or use a computer like my parents told me about from when they were kids—we were just expected to know how to use computers.

I was fine, having grown up on a computer thanks to my tech-savvy Dad, but many kids were not. Somebody took it all out of the curriculum by the time I got to school and I’m still seeing the ramifications of that to this day, even in a tech-oriented programming course.

Gen Z, at least in my curriculum, was not properly taught to use a computer. This generation is good on phones, but that’s about it—technological literacy, just like any kind of literacy—is not inherent and must be taught. Proper typing and computer literacy is actually quite lacking for a generation who is thought to be so chronically-online. It’s concerning that I see this not only in the arts, business and journalism programs, but also in my own very computer-heavy program—literally involving computer programming.

What’s relevant here is that instead of being given printed material, half the time we were given Chromebooks and told to read the material online. Even if the links weren’t broken and you could actually get to the site, most kids would just play web games instead of stare at the walls of text.

This was happening in fourth grade. You can probably imagine how much of a trainwreck it became once you got to high school and the widespread adoption of the smartphone and social media had been completed.

GEN Z AND READING IN 2026

Some Yes and Star Trek—not my favourite novel, but a nice photo.

I find myself in the final few weeks of my college program, and I know almost nobody who reads.

There are many who say they love to read, have stacks of books, but never touch them; they haven’t been able to get back into it. Phone and internet addiction is a real thing, and much of what we consume these days has probably conditioned us to have less patience for books.

There is a vast majority who—quite candidly—tell me they simply don’t think it’s fun and they’d rather just watch YouTube or Instagram Reels. They believe there is nothing to be gleaned or enjoyed from a book. I understand that books aren’t for everyone and not everybody reads all the time, but they don’t read anything, at all—fiction, non-fiction, recipe book or even a manual.

I used to scoff at people would say that comic books and manga is reading—I like comic books, but I don't think of it as literature. Now, people tell me they don't even read those anymore. Books, comic books and manga compilations have become just "art books": they're decorative and serve no function—their contents were never meant to be enjoyed when purchased.

There are much more efficient ways of getting information or entertainment: short-form content and now AI summaries being two big hitters.

There is an odd stench in the air whenever reading comes up in conversation, too. I carry a book around with me pretty much wherever I go, and I’ll often get weird looks about it amongst those who don’t know me well. I read at the bus shelter, on the bus, before class, during breaks, at lunch and whenever there is time to fill. I’ll plop myself down in a commons area, a coffee shop, the library or on a park bench and just enjoy a book.

Often, I get comments of people asking if I “actually read”; they’re surprised that I “read for real.” It was very confusing until someone accused me of being “performative” and I stumbled across a trend where people seemingly pretend to read in order to attract women.

Let me tell you, reading certifiably does not get you bitches. I read fifty-three books last year—trust me on this one.

Some of my favourite books of 2025: A Farewell To Arms, Wuthering Heights, Pride and Prejudice, Persuasion and Far From The Madding Crowd

I find it extraordinarily odd that the cultural default seems to be that reading is not normal; it’s more likely for someone to lie when they stay they read or to pretend to read than it is to actually just read a book. Once you talk for a bit and flush the stench, people are curious and it’s nice to talk to non-readers about what I read and their feelings on it.

Amongst most people, reading is seen as an “old person thing” and something that’s fallen out of relevance. Many recognize the harm their smartphones are doing and like the idea of starting to read or getting back into reading, but ultimately, it’s just talk.

I joined my college’s book club and it was refreshing to be around people who read and to hear their perspectives on the book, though there were only five people. One of them reads with the same fervor as me, while the others are more casual readers.

A long-time friend recently got back into reading and she’s steadily plodded along through a couple of books after having not read for a couple years. Another friend seemingly tried and failed—but there was effort. A former coworker also read almost as much as I do.

These are the people in my generation who read books. I’m not a super social guy, but I do talk to a lot of people and I’m pretty well-liked wherever I go. This is not a lot of people.

To throw in another wrench in the equation, only one of those people is actually another guy—he’s in the book club, and from what I know, he’s only more of a casual or occasional reader. Basically, I’m the only dude I’ve met my age who reads books.

Where are the dudes? Why does carrying or being adjacent to a book suddenly odd, seemingly sticking out like a sore thumb? Reading is supposed to be a normal part of society—or at least I thought it was for a long time before I was around. It doesn’t make sense that I should stand out—for the lack of a better word—because I’m holding a book or reading in public. It sounds absurd just writing it!

On the bus, there are plenty of middle-aged men who read books in transit, though the grand majority of transit riders are on their phones scrolling or gaming. These guys read library books or hardcovers with the jackets left on. There is occasionally girls my age on the bus reading books—mostly paperbacks, while one or two have e-readers.

I’ve only ever seen one guy reading a book on the bus, and I’d say that he aligns with what I found during my research of a “performative male.”

JUST DOING MY THING

Hell yeah!

It all sounds a little hopeless for books, doesn’t it?

I have no idea how they’re teaching reading skills in school, and I’d guess the next generation of kids will have an even more difficult time now that they’re growing up with even more exposure to digital candy at even younger ages.

People my age are quickly realizing the harm that social media and smartphones are doing, and will change their ways—but how will they fill their time? It’s yet to be seen in what form this will calcify. Even when we put our phones down, nobody seems to be interested in the written word.

I read the local bi-weekly newspaper whenever it comes out, but I’ve never actually seen anyone under seventy read a newspaper in public. When I can, I’ll acquire a copy of The Toronto Star and read through the thick, premium bundle of curated reading. Newsprint or conventional news outlets are far from the first place people look for their news these days, preferring to trust unverified social media accounts which may or may not uphold the principals of journalism.

Me? I’m just sitting somewhere—or standing—with my book, doing my own thing. I love reading; at the time of writing, I’ve read twenty-and-a-half books this year in mid-March. It’s encouraging to meet more and more people who want to read—it’s just a matter of seeing how many translate into actual readers. I’ll be here doing my thing whether they do or not, but it’d be nice to have some company.
