I’ve seen *Star Wars* at least a dozen times throughout my life, and I’ve watched pretty much every cut of it out there—but never on the big screen. When my local theatre, The Mayfair, announced they’d be showing it, I knew I just had to go. 

### Arrival

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-05-04_1.webp"><span>A massive french poster for the movie displayed in the snack bar</span></div>

After around an hour of rock climbing, I hopped off the wall and rode the bus up to Bank St. Already, forty minutes before the movie, a line was forming outside the theatre: people of all ages answered the call. Instead of leaving the doors open like they usually do, they kept the crowd outside and opened doors at around a half hour to showtime. 

As I reached the door, I looked behind me to see the line wrapping around the block past the Shoppers Drug Mart and down a side-street. Many bought advance tickets and were scanned in at the door, but I went to the booth and bought my ticket the old fashioned way with my membership. 

I claimed a seat by leaving my jacket and went off to get a popcorn and drink. The theatre teemed with joy and a tangible excitement crackling in the air. The concessions line was longer than it ever has been; for *Casablanca*, it wrapped around the stairs. Tonight, with fifteen minutes till showtime, the line snaked up the stairs, down the aisle and around the front row of seats across the screen. 

What an event *Star Wars* is! A gathering like this really makes you feel how much of an impact the movie and franchise has had on society; it’s really special to a lot of people, and it was so nice to be surrounded by a handful of them. 

The movie, unsurprisingly, did not start on time. At 18:35, a familiar man—one of the proprietors of the theatre, I suppose—came up to the front to make an announcement. He wore a “MacClunkey” shirt. 

The man, charismatic and enthusiastic, apologized for the delay and gave us some perspective on the screening. Since they took over in 2008, the current owners of the Mayfair have never screened *Star Wars* before—despite the large amount of memorabilia spread throughout the theatre. 

Because of the “Disney Vault”—the company’s artificial scarcity strategy—Star Wars hadn’t been screened at the Mayfair since around 1997 or 1998. Due to recent changes, they were finally able to get access to it, allowing this weekend’s screenings to take place. 

He announced that it was officially sold out—something like three-hundred twenty-five people were packed into the theatre, at least a quarter of them waiting in the concessions line which was all the way down to the piano. He apologized again, said a couple jokes, got some laughs and applause from the crowd, and told us that we’d get underway in around five minutes. 

It was in that moment that the hodgepodge gathering of people became one. 

### The Movie

<div class="blog-image blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-05-04_2.webp"><span>The poster at the door advertizing the trilogy's Special Edition re-release</span></div>

The previews came and went, the lights turned off and long, long ago in a galaxy far, far away, the iconic yellow STAR WARS logo screamed into existence with the regal brilliance of John Williams’ score. 

It was cathartic. 

Three-hundred twenty-five people watched the yellow opening credits scroll into infinity, bags of popcorn fluttering like leaves in the wind. 

Just like that, we were underway and I was back amongst familiar faces, but it felt…different. 

This was the 1997 cut of Star Wars—it was the version of the film I first watched with my Dad when I was just three or four years old—but I have never seen *Star Wars* like this. 

Everything is different when you see it on the big screen. I felt this when I saw *Logan’s Run* a couple months ago, but that was a movie I’d only seen one other time. I thought I knew *Star Wars*, and I do, but this was an insanely different experience!

The theatre was alive with joy and love for the experience. We all laughed along with the jokes—some of which, I’ll admit, I laughed a second or two early before they were actually spoken aloud. Many cried when Obi-Wan was struck down by Vader, and the sniffles returned when Biggs was taken out during the trench run. When the lights came on at the end, there was a long, loud applause. It was a communal experience. It felt fantastic. 

The movie was beautiful in its original 2.35:1 aspect ratio. I feel like I’d never seen the movie before, and I suppose that in many ways, I haven’t. Just like *THX 1138*, *Star Wars* is shot at very shallow focal lengths, leading to lots of beautiful shots with dreamy, swirly bokeh. You can even see the lens breathing in some of the quick focus pulls!

George Lucas made use of all the empty space when framing a scene, and I’m once again blown away at how good this movie looks and feels. It’s easy to dismiss *Star Wars* as some nerdy over-inflated franchise, but man, Lucas is a really talented filmmaker and storyteller. Having recently seen *THX 1138*—also at the Mayfair—I appreciate *Star Wars* and its creator even more.  

### Go watch Star Wars!

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-05-04_3.webp"><span>A shot of the theatre after the movie. (Nikon Z6)</span></div>

Like I said, I’ve seen *Star Wars* quite a few times. The original movie is my favourite; I’ve seen the Blu-Ray release, the 1997 cut on DVD and the original retail release on VHS—the latter of which I’ve watched the most out of them all. 

My Dad once told me that the home video releases are nothing like seeing it in theatres—he saw the original cut in 1977—and he’s absolutely right. I would highly recommend seeing it in theatres if you’ve got the chance. 

Next year, for the fiftieth anniversary of the film, they’re planning on re-releasing the original 1977 cut in theatres. I’m really looking forward to seeing that. So is my Dad. 

I’m glad that *Star Wars* exists and that I was able to enjoy it with so many people at my local theatre. 

May the force be with you, reader.
