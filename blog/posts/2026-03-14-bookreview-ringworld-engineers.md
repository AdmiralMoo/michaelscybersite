I spent this morning finishing the last hundred and fifty pages of The Ringworld Engineers. I’m devastated, to say the least. The storytelling of this novel was much more high-stakes than the first one; instead of a juvenile, curious romp across a planet, Larry Niven put the fate of the Ringworld itself in the hands of Louis Wu and Chmee, formerly Speaker-To-Animals. 

They were abducted by Hindmost—the former ruler of the Pierson’s Puppeteers and the mate of Nessus, the puppeteer who organized the first expedition to Ringworld. Throughout the course of the novel, the reader is introduced to the strange and otherworldly species inhabiting the ring. Louis befriends them, helping resolve their troubles in exchange for information on the mysterious Ringworld Protectors. 

The Ringworld itself is as colossal as before—a ribbon of a trillion Earths wrapped around a distant star. Now, this megastructure—the home of thirty trillion beings—is under threat and must be physically set back into place. The scale and concepts are simply mind-blowing. 

The stakes mount higher and higher as Niven introduces lore from his Known Space shared universe, revealing that the Pak—a now-extinct species—are the progenitors of the human race, the hominids of the Ringworld and the builders of the Ringworld itself, tying up many loose ends from the first book explaining the oddly human-compatible nature of the world. 

The book references the Protectors, Phssthpok and Jack Brennan—characters from his 1973 novel Protector. Although the first book already referenced concepts from his Known Space universe, this sequel will surely tease and hook you into reading the rest of his works to dive deeper into the lore of this amazing universe. 

It’s a glorious, imaginative, vivid sequel which successfully utilizes the original body of work as a springboard into a rich and fleshed-out universe. I greatly enjoyed this book, and the ending had me absolutely reeling—I wasn’t expecting it to become so heavy and serious at the end, but it did and I respect it more for that. 

I waited eleven months to read this sequel because I wanted to find a physical copy of the book. I stumbled across one four days ago and started reading it right away. Just as with the first Ringworld book, I feel like a whole month has passed. This is a fun read for anyone who likes good sci-fi.
