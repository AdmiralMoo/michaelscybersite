March 2026 was truly the month that *5150* came alive. At the end of February, I was sitting in one of the common spaces at college reading a book about newspapers when I had the odd urge to listen to this Van Hagar album I'd only heard once a couple years ago. Since July 17th, 2024, this album has sat unappreciated in my collection. This month, *I sensed a change, nothin' felt the same, all my dreams felt strange and love came walkin' in*.

I absolutely love the power and fervor this album comes at you with; Sammy Hagar's extraordinary screaming vocals and Eddie's legendary riffs mixed with slick, glossy synths is such a pleasure to my ears. I truly believe that this is the best Van Halen album of them all—none of them have this kind of power, urgency and sincerity wrapped into one beautiful bundle. 

After that, we had Nickelback's *Silver Side Up*: a short, fun album that gets too much undeserved hate. Throughout February, I listened to songs off this album piecemeal on playlists while I skated around in circles—they're good sing-along songs. I was doing some schoolwork set up in a shopping mall one day when I decided to listen to this one in full and it really clicked. 

*The Cars* is one I picked up at a thrift store a while ago and got around to listening to it for the first time. It's laid back and pretty catchy—some simple 1980s New Wave fun. *Breaking Benjamin/Ember* is an album on its way up—I think it'll be trending towards a renaissance in the month of April. I *loved* this album when it came out back in 2018, and I still can't believe it's been almost eight years since it was released. 

*Hotel California*, *4* and *Out of Time* are largely carry-over from last month; I'm still really into them so they've found themselves in semi-regular rotation for a second month in a row. *Making Movies* is the most recent and final Dire Straits album I added to my collection, so I'm still getting familiar with it. It's such a pleasure to dive into one of these, though this one is bittersweet since there are no more unfamiliar Dire Straits albums after this. 

Finally, we've got *Grateful Dead/American Beauty* and *Pink Floyd/Meddle* making top-ten appearances on this month's listening charts. They're solid albums; I've always got some Pink Floyd going, and there's a whole mix of it in the full monthly ranking—the same goes for The Grateful Dead. Further down the order, there's Boston's *Walk On* just missing out on the top ten along with Rush's *Permanent Waves*, *Eric Clapton/Slowhand*, *Genesis/Invisible Touch*, *Billy Joel/52nd Street*, *Phil Collins/But Seriously*, *Third Eye Blind/Third Eye Blind*, *Genesis/Selling England By The Pound*, *Dire Straits/Dire Straits*, *Derek And The Dominos/Layla And Other Assorted Love Songs*, *Creed/Human Clay*, *Creed/My Own Prison* and The Footloose Soundtrack (of all things) competing for twelfth place with four listens a piece. What a list!

Although my stats aren't completely refined for this month, initial reports indicate that I listened to 148 unique albums 326 times for a total of 13,831 minutes.

### Two Discographies Completed

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_1.webp"><span>The Genesis section of my shelf</span></div>

This month, I completed two bands' studio album discographies: *Creed* and *Genesis*.

I'm a big Genesis fan—I mean, this site has a Genesis Fanpage! Having helped define the Prog sound of the 1970's and the catchy synth gloss of the 1980s, I think they're one of the best bands to ever do it. I love their early stuff with Peter Gabriel, and I love their later stuff with Phil Collins: they're two different worlds—two different bands, almost—but equally enjoyable.

I've been amassing Genesis albums since November 16th, 2023, when I found a copy of *Invisible Touch* and *We Can't Dance*. On March 14th, 2026, I walked into a record store I don't usually go to and found a copy of *Trespass* used. 

Genesis—in all their forms—has been a staple of my music listening rotation for the past couple of years, and there's no sign of slowing down. The more Genesis, the better!

Creed is a band that I've loved for a long time—my Dad has always enjoyed the band, so I grew up with them. *My Own Prison* was one of the first twenty albums I bought when I first started my CD library back in 2019, followed soon after by *Human Clay*. Creed's first three albums are relatively easy to find at thrift stores because they sold well and were quickly disowned like pets during the COVID pandemic. 

You can't say the same thing about their fourth and (at the moment) final album, *Full Circle*. 

Commercially, it was a failure compared to their previous efforts, but I think it kicks just as much ass and takes as many names as their first three. After writing a blog post about them, I decided it was time to get a copy of that elusive fourth album. 

It arrived from Spain via Discogs on the final day of the month, March 31st. Hell yeah!

### Late-Career Album Appreciation

That brings us to the second trend of the month. I spent much of February listening to Boston's fantastic and forgotten fourth album, *Walk On*, which put me in the mood for more late-career albums like that one which aren't as bad as people think they are. 

When I walked into the record store on March 9th, I had Mötley Corabi and Van Cherone on the mind: two albums I'm somewhat familiar with from my streaming days almost ten years ago, but I haven't found copies of. Lo and behold, there they were! *Mötley Crüe/Mötley Crüe* and *Van Halen/Van Halen III* were sitting right there for me in the "New Arrivals" bin. 

I think *Van Halen III* isn't the best Van Halen album, but it's got good moments on it. Mötley Crüe's self-titled record doesn't deserve any of the hate it gets and I wish they put out more music with John Corabi. I love how filthy and heavy those riffs and gritty vocals are—you can say the same thing for Creed's *Full Circle*, another late-career album that's fallen by the wayside. 

Listening to and loving Van Hagar, Mötley Corabi, *Full Circle* and *Walk On* amongst others, you've really got to wonder: what the hell is wrong with people? Are they even listening to the music, or are they just reacting to change and acting out in prejudice? 

### Early Albums

On the opposite side of the spectrum, I picked up quite a few albums from earlier in artists' careers. I'm not as familiar with early Fleetwood Mac as I would like to be, and I'm excited to get into *Penguin* and especially *Future Games* knowing Clapton, McVie and Fleetwood were involved in the Bluesbreakers. 

*Toto/Toto* is an exciting find—I picked up their debut album at the monthly library book sale along with those Fleetwood albums. The *Huey Lewis* bunch are also exciting additions; I guess I'm getting old enough that I'm embracing Adult Contemporary. Blame that on Phil Collins' solo work!

Of course, *Trespass* is Genesis' second album, and the first of the Peter Gabriel era after their experimental college-aged debut. 

*Journey to The Centre of the Eye* is the debut album from English Progressive Rock band Nektar. I'd never heard of them before, but I saw it in the "New Arrivals" bin on the same day as I found the Mötley Crüe and Van Halen albums. I flipped through the booklet and it radiated Progressive Rock—I just had to check it out. I thoroughly enjoyed the album—you can check out my thoughts on the Music Recommendations page!

### Massive Tape Haul

The biggest thing this month is the massive haul of cassette tapes. I bring these home every so often, but I don't think I've ever walked out of a thrift store with this many at once! 

I could not believe my eyes when I saw so many albums and bands I'm interested in browsing through the shopping baskets of tapes at my main thrift store. Usually, it's a bunch of country and Christian music compilations with the odd audiobook thrown in; someone cool must've died or downsized their collection. This guy didn't write his name anywhere; he just left a signature as well as the index number of each tape—I suppose he had them all on a list.

As a part of this tape haul, I got my first Sammy Hagar and Eddie Money albums (the former is a compilation). I've enjoyed Hagar as a part of Van Halen and Eddie Money's hit singles, but I've never delved deeper into their discographies as solo artists. 

It's fantastic to have a copy of *52nd Street* on tape—that's my favourite Billy Joel album, and quite easily one of my favourites of all-time. I'm happy to have two more Styx albums at my disposal; interestingly, this is the second time I've found two Styx albums on tape at a thrift store in pairs—the last time was back in October. 

On the flip side, *In The Dark* is a very exciting find—I bought that album last October on tape from a record store and it became one of my most-listened to albums that month. I'm very happy to finally have that one on CD.
