In September of 2018, I finally convinced my Dad to let me have his old camera. It’d sat unused for five years since he upgraded to a newer one, but after I picked it up, I wouldn’t set it down for another seven years until it took its final shot. 

Now, eleven months on from the day it died, I wanted to write about my first camera—the venerable Nikon D40—and how it compares to the relatively modern Z6.  

### MY D40 – ORIGIN STORY & ITS TIME WITH ME

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_15.webp"><span>A view from the primate house at the Toronto Zoo in 2023</span></div>

The D40 is an entry level camera—the cheapest thing you could buy at the time to enter digital interchangeable lens photography. My Dad lost his beloved Canon AE-1—his first “real” camera, and the one he used throughout the 1980s and 1990s—when moving house in 2001. Though he liked Canon, Nikon was the only option since the D40’s kit was around $700 and the comparable Canon EOS 30D package over $1000. 

With it, he documented my childhood; mundane days, summer afternoons on the beach, Toronto Zoo trips, Blue Jays games—he brought it everywhere. In 2008, he bought the 55–200mm zoom so he could take it with him to Budapest—his job’s year-end teambuilding exercise was (somehow) the Hungarian Grand Prix. 

From 2006 to 2014, he shot 13,000 photos with this camera and brought it all around Europe. Then, he got a job at Canon. 

Through employee discounts and performance bonuses, he was able to get himself a Rebel T5 kit, a zoom and a 50mm prime for next to nothing. That was the end of his journey with the D40, which served faithfully. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_10.webp"><span>This is what leaves looked like in 2023</span></div>

In September of 2018, it’d been sitting around collecting dust for some time and he decided I was old and responsible enough to have it for my own. I took it out on walks every day down to the waterline and documented where I lived and grew up. I didn’t watch any tutorials or read the manual, I just went out and shot in full manual and had a blast. 

This camera came with me moving house twice, I brought it to events in high school, I took it to air shows, on road trips, vacations and every conceivable place that’d let me in with a camera. It was simply a part of me for seven long years—a constant in a tumultuous time of my life: if Michael was there, he probably had his camera. 

I loved this camera a lot, and it kept on giving the more I loved it. It was comfortable in my hand as if it were an extension of me—the tactile controls felt smooth and natural as anything.  

I bought a *Sigma APO 150-500mm f/5-6.3* in 2023 so that I could get better reach at air shows. I got into birding, and I also took it to a couple races—the 2024 Canadian Tire Grand Prix at Mosport, and the 2024 Sahlen’s Six Hours of the Glen at Watkins Glen. 

When I got the Series E 35mm, it pretty much completely replaced the 18–55mm kit lens. I enjoyed shooting full manual with a nice, tactile aperture ring.

For the majority of the time I had the camera, I knew that it was going to die. I was surprised it’d gotten that far considering how much I shot with it. I treated it well, but I experimented a lot and I took a lot of photos every day. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_16.webp"><span>The DeHavilland Dash 8-400: my D40's final shot. (kidding—this is a MiG-17F at an airshow in 2024)</span></div>

On May 3rd, 2025, I was sitting on a rock in the middle of a pond reading *Mansfield Park* by Jane Austen. The beaver I was waiting for surfaced and I raised the camera, quickly composed and spun dials, and gently pressed the shutter button—CRUNCH!

"Error: Press Shutter Release Button Again”

And that was that: the shutter was gone; the camera, dead. 

For a moment, anyways. 

I turned it on and off, and nothing happened. I took the lens off and did something I’ve never done: I stuck my finger in and wiggled the stuck shutter blades gently. They retracted when I clicked the shutter again. 

Like Nikki Sixx in 1987 or Jesus on the third day, the D40 was back alive. It only lasted three or four shots, and it died again. The last photo I took with it was of a DeHavilland Dash 8-400—C-FPVJ operated by PAL Airlines. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-04_1.png"><span>The final shutter count of my D40, via <i>camerashuttercount.com</i></span></div>

The D40, like I said, is an entry level camera; its shutter is rated for only 50,000 shots, but mine lasted for a whopping 209,365.

195,363 were my photos, and 14,002 were my Dad’s. 

### SPECS & KIT – A CAMERA FROM ANOTHER AGE

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_12.webp"><span>One of my favourite photos I've ever taken: a magical sunset at my favourite sunset spot in Ottawa</span></div>

By the time 2018 rolled around, the D40 was—by all definitions—an outdated dinosaur. In fact, two months after I got the D40, Nikon launched their first mirrorless cameras: the Z6 and Z7. 

Launched in November of 2006, the D40 hails from the very beginning of the DSLR era: at its core lies a humble 6.1-Megapixel CCD sensor with a very grainy ISO range of 200–3200, and a shutter capable of 1/4000 at 2.5 frames per second. 

It’s very much a film camera with a sensor packed inside and a display slapped on the back; when shooting in Manual as I always did, you’re left alone to trust your light meter, know your exposure triangle and shoot happily without the aids or distractions of a computer. 

At the centre of the viewfinder are three autofocus points selectable using the directional pad on the back of the camera body—the active one flashes red during focusing. Underneath the frame is a lime green readout showing the shutter speed, a focus indicator dot, aperture, light meter, exposure compensation, a low-battery warning and the amount of photos left. With SD card capacities limited to 32GB, you’d have roughly 3,000 RAW photos per card. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_5.webp"><span>Somewhere down Spadina</span></div>

The viewfinder itself is very small compared to full frame, and it’s not nearly as bright—though I didn’t have any other frame of reference while I had the camera, so it never bothered me. This is because the sensor is roughly half the size of a full frame 35mm sensor—these early DSLRs used the APS-C format. 

Because of this, every lens has a 1.5x “crop factor”—35mm becomes a 52mm lens, 200mm becomes 300mm, and so on. The kit lens bundled with the camera—the cheap, plasticky *AF-S DX Nikkor f/3.5-5.6G 18–55mm*—is actually a 27–82.5mm. The D40 came bundled with the original version of this lens, not the one with vibration reduction. I also had access to the *Nikkor AF-S DX 55–200mm f/4.0-5.6G ED VR*. 

Both lenses are completely plastic and weigh next to nothing. I could go on and on about how they’re really not the sharpest or fastest things in the world, but they most certainly do the job. When you’re getting into photography—or even when you’re just enjoying photography and not being a spec snob—these are more than good enough. I used them for the first six years I had the camera until I bought a *Nikon Series E 35mm f/2.5*.

### CCD SENSOR MAGIC

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_18.webp"><span>Corny photo</span></div>

My favourite thing about the Nikon D40 was the way it rendered colours. There’s something a little bit funky and imperfect about it, but I love it. 

Nikon’s colour science back in the early 2000’s was still early days in the digital era, so it wasn’t as clinical as modern day CMOS sensors and colour profiles. This gave the D40 and other early DSLRs its own signature quality, almost like a film stock. 

It leaned towards reds, oranges, yellows and magentas—grass and trees, for example, would be pulled ever so slightly in this direction. Brick buildings really popped because of how much it favoured red. Just like how shadows on Kodak Portra 400 become a bit greenish, the shadows on the D40’s sensor would become magenta-ish. 

Every colour was vivid and slightly off in one direction or another which gave it a very lush and full appearance. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_9.webp"><span>A dreamy springtime sunset</span></div>

In particular, I loved the way it rendered the sky. The vast blue expanse glowed and was always pulled slightly towards purple—that almost unreal shade you see when you’ve been swimming in a pool for a while and you look up. 

Naturally, there was a lot of noise even at low ISOs. I tried to shoot at ISO 200 most of the time, going up to ISO 1600 only during the night and ISO 800 during high-speed action shots which needed stopped-down apertures. ISO 3200 was almost completely unusable in most situations until Lightroom introduced their AI Denoise feature. 

I would estimate that in terms of noise levels, ISO 200 was equivalent to ISO 800 on the Nikon Z6. The latter’s ISO 3200 is more like ISO 800 on the D40. I’m constantly blown away by how good high ISOs are on modern cameras—if you see other people on the Net telling beginners to shoot as low ISO as possible, it’s because they had to deal with early DSLRs!

### THE D40 AS A BEGINNER CAMERA

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_19.webp"><span>After a storm across the drainage pond</span></div>

The D40 was simple and a joy to use. It was a very lightweight body, though I was always confident it could be used as a weapon of self defense if needed. Six megapixels is more than enough if you’re not shooting in a professional capacity—and even if you are, it’s a perfectly fine place to start. A lot of people thought I had a super expensive camera when I showed them my photos and I took pleasure in telling them it was something you could get on Facebook Marketplace for $40. 

It was especially nice talking to other people who were new to photography; there’s too much talk about specs on the internet, and everybody seems to have forgotten that photography is about taking photos, and you just need a sealed box with some photosensitive element to capture light. Do people forget that many still shoot film? 

I’m glad that I learned photography on the D40; I think it’s the perfect camera for learning to take photos. It’s cheap and easy to develop photos, and if you want it, you can get instant feedback on what you just shot using the back screen. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_20.webp"><span>My favourite kind of flowers: Marigolds</span></div>

There are no distractions and there are no crutches. There are consequences for using high ISOs and you have to expose correctly to have something to work with during the digital development process. The readout in the viewfinder is simple and only tells you what you need.

I think that modern sensors are great; BSI CMOS sensors’ ability to see in the dark and crank up to absurd ISOs is fantastic, and in body image stabilization is like magic. I don’t, however, think those are good tools to have in your toolbox if you’re just starting out with photography. I think they’re great as upgrades, but I wouldn’t have learned to hold a camera properly if I had IBIS the whole time, nor would I have actually learned to understand photography as well as I do had my camera not had limits. 

The D40 is a camera, and that’s all it is. I think that is its biggest strength. 

### THE OLD VS THE NEW – D40 VS Z6

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_3.webp"><span>Somewhere below Toronto...</span></div>

After the D40 died, I bought a refurbished Nikon Z6 with around 3,000 shutter actuations for a little over $1,000 CAD. The one I got came bundled with the FTZ adapter, so I was able to pick up right where I left off with my beloved Series E lenses. 

It’s not the same, and for a while, I was unhappy with it. It took a couple of months for me to get used to it, and I’ve only really started loving the photos I’m taking with it in the past three or four months. The Z6 is twelve years newer than the D40, and it’s like an F-86 Sabre versus an F-35 Lightning II—it does the same basic task, but it’s so technologically advanced and there’s *so much* computer. 

As I said, I loved the old Nikon colour science on the D40’s CCD sensor, but I’ve been able to achieve a look close to that in Lightroom based on identical photos taken by my Nikon D60 and Z6 side-by-side. The Z6’s CMOS sensor has its own signature look, though, and I’ve learned to appreciate that. 

Although I rarely use autofocus lenses—and this might be the core issue—I think it’s a bit confusing and overdone on the Z6. It feels like there’s an excessive amount of autofocus points when I would rather have my three points so I can focus and recompose. Sometimes, it just feels like the menus are so deep and there’s so much *stuff* that I’m holding too much computer and not enough camera. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_8.webp"><span>The beautiful, legendary P-51D Mustang</span></div>

My only real complaint about the Z6—and a rather silly gripe I don’t think will ever go away—is the viewfinder. To me, it sucks. I actively dislike the electronic viewfinder, and would much prefer an optical viewfinder any day of the week—even in the dark. 

It’s convenient, but I don’t like that I can see the final image through the viewfinder as settings are applied. I know you can turn off “Live View”, but the alternative is just as bad—the camera tries to auto-expose and adjust the viewfinder’s brightness, so either way, you can’t really understand or trust the light meter. Maybe that’s just me—maybe it’s a skill issue. 

I just don’t like looking at a little TV screen when I take photos; I don’t like the flashing overlays and I don’t like how flat and lifeless it feels compared to an optical viewfinder where you’re actually seeing through the glass. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-04-01_11.webp"><span>Another one of my favourite black and white photos</span></div>

Ultimately, that’s just something I’ve gotten used to and have to accept regardless; times change, and so does technology. I still have a D60 and an FM for when I want a more analog experience—there are cameras for every situation. I hate sounding like my steak is too juicy and my lobster too buttery, and I actually love using the Z6 day-to-day almost a year on…it’s just a completely different experience. 

It’s odd that I’m comparing the D40 favourably to the high-tech menace that is the Z6, but I think it just speaks to how much I loved that camera, and how familiar I was with it. 

To me, there will never be another camera as perfect and simple as the Nikon D40.
