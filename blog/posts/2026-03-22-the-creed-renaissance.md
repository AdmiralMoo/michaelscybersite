###THE CREED RENAISSANCE

I don’t think anybody could have predicted the seismic shift of Creed’s perception in our collective conscience. If you told me five years ago that Creed was cool, I would’ve just laughed: for as long as I can remember, they were the internet’s second most-hated band. Creed-bashing—the act of hating on Creed for no apparent reason—was once commonplace in our culture.

Somehow, in the past two or three years, society’s attitude towards the band has completely shifted. The average person—even those who don’t listen to rock—know the big hits like “Higher” and “One Last Breath”. I realized how significant this shift was while watching qualifying for last year’s Indy 500—Rosenqvist’s No. 60 MSR had the band’s logo emblazoned on the side of the car!

It’s a fantastic time to live in; for those who love to rock, there is no longer any shame in liking this epic band. But what the hell happened? 

###LISTEN IN SHAME: A HATED BAND

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-22_4.webp"><span>The tracklisting of their first album, <i>My Own Prison</i>. My favourite track off this one—and probably my favourite Creed song of all—is "Torn".</span></div>

I have always listened to Creed. 

Growing up, there was always Creed in circulation around the house because my Dad liked them. I was exposed to a lot of *Human Clay* along with many songs off their other two albums, *My Own Prison* and *Weathered*.

Some of the first albums I bought with my own money on CD were Creed—*Human Clay* and *My Own Prison* were quickly tracked down at thrift stores and acquired. All three have been staples of my music listening over the past seven years I’ve been collecting CD’s.

I have always enjoyed Creed. I never dunked on them or hated them like everybody else did; I stuck by them and sang along while others hated and dogpiled for seemingly no reason. 

I knew of this negative attitude from the internet, but didn’t encounter it in person until I got to high school. In my robotics class, the shop teacher had a radio which was never turned off, and was permanently tuned to the classic rock station. Between swaths of Boston, Led Zeppelin, AC/DC, Van Halen, Tragically Hip, Pearl Jam, Rush and many others, more and more Post-Grunge crept in from the contemporary rock stations. 

Three Days Grace, Breaking Benjamin, Three Doors Down, Staind, The Smashing Pumpkins and even Billy Talent all made it through accepted and unscathed. Whenever Creed and Nickelback came on, there was a riot in the class—twenty-nine teenage boys, some of which who were usually quiet—would cry out in hatred of those two bands, unable to give justification. The poor teacher would try to quell the crowd, but he couldn’t convince them otherwise. 

In conversation when you’d meet someone who listens to rock, such bands would come up and Creed would be dunked on unfairly. It made no sense, but the stench in the air was everywhere. 

It frustrated my Dad, too. He’d encountered people who didn’t like Creed, Three Doors Down and Nickelback, and has often exclaimed how awesome those bands are. 

Nobody knew I liked Creed, but I shamelessly continued rocking out—so did many middle-aged men. I never expected the tides to change as they did.  

###WHY DID EVERYBODY HATE CREED?

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-22_3.webp"><span>The tracklisting of their second album, <i>Human Clay</i>. I'd say this is their best album, though though their music is consistently really good. My favourite track off this one is "Faceless Man" or "Say I".</span></div>

From what I’ve gathered, many people thought Creed was just cookie-cutter rock music meant for the radio. They said it had no soul, and there was nothing unique about it. 

In-person, many people couldn’t really give solid reasons as to why they didn’t like the band. I think that by the time I was a teenager, there’d already been a fifteen year buildup of Creed hate built-up in society, primarily from the internet. 

Looking back on old posts and articles, it seems that fundamentally, Creed were destroyed by their own success. They became one of the biggest bands in North America for a while and heavy airplay across numerous hits wore the public down. 

After a decade of Grunge, the many didn’t take too kindly to Stapp’s gravely, growling vocals mixed with religious imagery; many labelled him a failed Eddie Vedder impersonator. 

Their rock was polished and made for the radio with generally uplifting or hope-toned lyrics which might have been regarded as “soft” or “hollow” compared to the comparatively depressing lyrics of the 1990’s and the hard-rocking party and love-ballad lyrics of the 1980’s. 

Towards the end of their original run, Scott Stapp’s mental health took a turn for the worse and his live performances degraded sharply, further contributing to their reputation as a bad band. 

###WHY DOES EVERYBODY LIKE CREED?

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-22_2.webp"><span>The tracklisting of their third album, <i>Weathered</i>. My favourite track off this one is "One Last Breath". Unfortunately, I don't have a copy of <i>Full Circle</i>, but I'm keeping my eyes peeled.</span></div>

The answer is simple: after years of immature, senseless hate, society’s balls dropped collectively. People grew up. And that’s actually closer to the truth than it sounds. 

There are a lot of reasons which have contributed to their resurgence in popular culture, probably starting with nostalgia. 

In 2027, it will have been thirty years since their debut album, *My Own Prison*, was released. We’re floating in the 20–30 year anniversary for the band’s music releases and cultural relevance, so there was bound to be a resurgence. With that long between now and then, the temperature has cooled and what was once overplayed is now fresh again. 

Creed, just like the bands of the 1980’s, will now be integrated into our popular culture as a “classic” and just another band which was really popular at the time. The next generation of Creed listeners won’t remember the hate like I do, and they’ll just be…a band. 

In the same vein as the balls-dropping joke, people have actually grown up and become less insecure about what they listen to. We live in an age where there is no more popular music—everything is popular and everything is relevant due to extreme individualism, online niches and the distancing from external content aggregators such as radio stations.

That set us up for the catalysts which set off this change.  

In 2023, the Texas Rangers (spits on the ground) used the song “Higher” heavily throughout their post-season run, possibly seeding the band back into the consciousness of the general public. I was surprised to hear Creed played during MLB and IndyCar broadcasts throughout 2025.

The primary driving force behind this change is social media and the mysterious algorithm. Creed got caught up in some kind of trend which proliferated enough positive opinion of the band for their perception to change, and even for their popularity to grow. 

I hear more Creed than ever this year at the bar, gym and bowling alleys on campus, and pretty much everyone there knows the lyrics. Even people who don’t listen to rock music know these songs because they apparently cycle around so much on social media. 

We’re living in *The Creed Renaissance* Era—and it’s important to understand that this isn’t some rediscovery. Creed didn’t suddenly become good—they always were: people just finally stopped acting like they weren’t.
