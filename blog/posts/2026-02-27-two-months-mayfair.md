Almost two months ago, I was walking south down Bank St. returning from the New Years fireworks at Lansdowne when I saw a poster for *Logan’s Run (1976)* at the Mayfair Theatre. 

I’d heard of the Mayfair before in passing, but I’d never considered going before that—my idea of theatres had been ruined by years of disappointing experiences at large multiplex theaters. I was surprised they were showing what I thought to be a relatively unknown old Sci-Fi flick, and I gave it a chance. 

As I wrote in [my blog post](/blog/generated/2026-01-04-logans-run.html) from January 4th, 2026, I had an absolute blast. I said I planned to go back often, and I have. I thought it’d be a good time to collect my thoughts two months after discovering this magical place. 

###THE MOVIES & ATMOSPHERE AT THE MAYFAIR

<div class="blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-02-27_sally.webp"><span>The <i>When Harry Met Sally</i> poster outside the theatre</span></div>

Since *Logan’s Run*, I’ve been to the Mayfair another seven times. I went back almost two weeks later to see *When Harry Met Sally (1989)* and it was a very different and even more pleasant experience. 

I was surprised at how many people went to see *Logan’s Run*—the theatre was around half-full with people of all ages. When *Harry Met Sally* was a sold-out show and it was just fantastic; it felt like a communal experience when we all laughed together every other beat. It was a really good, heartwarming movie, but I think seeing it at this theatre really elevated the experience. 

I’ve never felt a sense of community when watching a movie. I’ve never been to a movie where everybody keeps off their phones, nobody is talking to one-another and we’re all just enjoying it together. When there’s a sad part, the feeling of the room drops; when there’s something funny, we all laugh together (and this was probably one of the funniest movies of all time). 

Although not every movie is sold-out nor are they all funny riots, that sense of togetherness stays—even if you’re just one of five at the most obscure late-night grindhouse releases.

<div class="blog-image blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-02-27_casablanca.webp"><span>The <i>Casablanca</i> poster outside the theatre</span></div>

I had a great experience watching *Scarlet Warning 666 (1974)* and I went to see it a second time the day after I watched *Tank Girl (1995)*. A couple days later, it was another sold out show with *Casablanca (1942)*. 

The *Rocky Horror Picture Show (1975)* was a very special screening; it was hosted by The Absent Friends, a shadow cast who perform and jeer while the movie goes in the background. They had everybody laughing, singing, dancing along and throwing toilet paper amongst other things. I don’t think I’ll ever be able to watch Rocky Horror on home video again knowing how it is when you watch it like that. 

Finally, a couple days ago, I watched *Scum of The Earth (1974)*—another obscure grindhouse film like *Scarlet Warning*. 

###THE THEATREGOING EXPERIENCE AT THE MAYFAIR

<div class="blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-02-27_toilet.webp"><span>A replica of the Zero Gravity Toilet instruction panel from <i>Space Odyssey</i>. So much Eurostile!</span></div>

After a couple shows, the already very pleasant staff became even more friendly as they recognized me. When I step into the theatre, it feels like I’m slipping into a whole other world—a safe and pleasant pocket of euphoric insanity. The real world melts away and I’m immersed in that now familiar old building with it’s Spanish-revival stucco walls and eloquent fascias.  

They’ve got all sorts of awesome memorabilia crammed in wherever you look, including scale models, action figures, replica signs (including instructions for the Zero Gravity Toilet from 2001 in the bathroom stall) and so much more—not just cool movie posters. It’s a space inhabited by people who just love everything about cinema. 

The people at the concession stand make sure your drink is fully topped off by waiting for the fizz to clear. The popcorn is legitimately buttery and (probably to my detriment) legitimately addictive. Even just the concessions themselves are so much more affordable than any other theatre I’ve been to. 

There’s value wherever you look, especially after you get the annual membership which drops most tickets down to just $8.50. If you’re going more than three times in a year, it’s a no-brainer! 

It’s not just the staff who make it pleasant: everybody who goes to the Mayfair wants to be there—they love it too. I’ve met so many people and had so many pleasant conversations with strangers while waiting in line for the popcorn or just waiting for the movie to start. That, too, is part of what makes it feel like a community. 

###OTHER THEATRES & A TECHNICAL ASIDE

Now, I mentioned earlier that I had a bad impression of movie theatres because of big multiplex brands such as Cineplex and Landmark. I still do, really—it’s become clear to me that independent theatres such as the Mayfair are both the past and future of cinemas. With declining attendance, closures and cutbacks, I can see why the time of big-name theatres is well and truly coming to an end. 

Those big-brands seem to believe that expensive gimmicks, add-ons and streaming rentals are the way to go, but they can’t bother to maintain their projectors and actually deliver a quality product. 

The last couple of times I’ve been to a Landmark or a Cineplex over the past five years, I’ve found that across multiple locations, the following conditions are almost always present:

-	Theatre is dirty
-	Sound is too loud and/or incomprehensible
-	Tickets and concessions are too expensive
-	Not enough selection
-	Projection quality is abysmal

It’s that last point which really irks me: when I pay to see a movie in theatre, I’m expecting mind-blowing, boobie-busting quality. It’s beyond disappointing to stare at a fuzzy projection, a warped or sagging screen, trapezoidal keystone calibration, uneven or dim images and even flickering frames—I can’t put to words how abysmal the experience is!

At Cineplex in particular, I’ve always found it difficult to even get the tickets—they don’t have people to talk to at the booths and the website is terribly slow and unresponsive. I don’t want to sound like too much of a negative Nelly or a complain-y Catherine, but it really feels like it just isn’t what it was 15 years ago.  

###THE MAYFAIR: EVERYTHING THAT WAS EVER GOOD ABOUT CINEMAS

<div class="blog-image blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-02-27_food.webp"><span>Large popcorn (overflowing) and a large drink (filled to the brim).</span></div>

I’m not saying it’s all doom and gloom when you go to the Cineplex—I actually had a pretty fun time seeing *Pink Floyd At Pompeii – MCMLXXII (2025)* and *Roger Waters: This Is Not a Drill (2025)*. But if I’ve seen ten movies in five years but I was only happy coming out of two of them, and now I’ve seen eight movies in two months and I’m beyond elated, you can probably understand why I feel so passionately about this. 

The Mayfair is capable of 35mm projection, but I haven’t been to any films which explicitly advertise it. Regardless, every single time, the quality has been absolutely gorgeous. You could count the hairs on Sally Albright’s head and feel like you’re actually there. It’s stunning seeing such old movies in such high quality on such a big screen; it’s as if it were recorded yesterday!

Even *Scum of The Earth*—an extremely low-budget movie shot on the worse film imaginable—looked really good for what it was. Some of the scenes were shot at a very high ISO (ASA?) and they were very grainy, but it looked the way it was meant to; the grains themselves were sharp and it felt like you were watching the best-quality version of that movie available. 

It was actually very pleasant to see the halation, grain and dust in that movie. It, and *Tank Girl*, really showed off how bright the projection is, too. Both movies had lots of night scenes, but you could always make out what’s going on. 
In short, none of the pitfalls I mentioned about those big-box theatres are present at the Mayfair. It’s a clean, quality experience every single time. 

###YOU SHOULD GO TO THE MOVIES

If you told me last year that I regularly go to the movies a couple times a month, I wouldn’t believe you. I wouldn’t think that I could afford a $20 ticket, a $5 order fee and a $15 popcorn; I wouldn’t think I’d find the experience worthwhile every single time, nor would I think there were really that many movies to see. 

I’m overjoyed that independent theatres such as the Mayfair exist. It’s simple, cheap and a real pleasure to go to every time. It’s obvious that the people who run the theatre really care—you can feel it every step of the way.  

The Mayfair will continue to be a regular stop for me. It’s well worth checking out if you’re in the Ottawa area and you’re looking for a fun way to spend the night. This place reminded—and taught—me what going to the movies is supposed to feel like.
