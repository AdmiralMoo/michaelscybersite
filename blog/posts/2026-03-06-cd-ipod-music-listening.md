I haven’t streamed music in well over three years at the time of writing, and I haven’t looked back. When I’m at home, I’ve got my ever-growing CD collection and when I’m out and about, I’ve got an iPod. I never have to deal with internet connectivity issues, downloading songs and having to pay monthly subscriptions. 

It sounds fantastic, doesn’t it? Well, it is—but I don’t think it’s for everyone. 

###THE STREAMING PROBLEM

<div class="blog-image blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_1940_01.webp"><span>A stack of albums I listened to often in the Spring of 2025</span></div>

Growing up, I listened to music from my parents’ CD collection and iTunes libraries. I was born on the tail-end of the Compact Disc’s rule; the format’s dominance blended into the short-lived iTunes era before streaming became the endgame for music-listening. 

Streaming, for all practical intents and purposes, is a very convenient way to listen to music. People who want to listen to a song don’t have to buy an entire album or even pay per individual track to get their fix. After my parents gave away their CDs and iTunes no longer made sense, I used the free tier of Spotify before subscribing and becoming a premium user for a while. 

At first, it seemed great; almost every song you could imagine right there at your fingertips. I added the albums I liked to my library and threw together a couple of playlists; it was great. 

Over time, I realized that I’m not the market audience for something like Spotify. I love music and I listen to it all day, every day, but I listen to full albums. Spotify is like a popularity-contest social media platform—it shoves recommendations in your face, tries to get you to watch vertical videos and doesn’t really care for the album experience. 

I never used the Discover Weekly playlists because none of it ever appealed to me; while others seem to enjoy setting it on autopilot and having the algorithm decide what they wanted to listen to, I’ve always been much more deliberate. Neither way is the right way, but when the entire app is built on that social media autopilot philosophy, it’s just not going to work out. 

Spotify’s compression algorithm doesn’t sound very good to me. To this day, you can download songs off the net and it’ll sound better than Spotify because it’s uncompressed or just straight up sourced off a CD. This isn’t a problem for most people, but it’s something I noticed after a while. 

Over time, record labels add new editions of albums—“Super Mega Deluxe” editions with forty-eight songs which really shouldn’t be there. It’s not convenient to have to make a playlist for each Super Mega Deluxe album with the original track-list—it starts to feel idiotic when you’re jumping through hoops like that. Add the fact that modern devices don’t have enough storage space to download entire albums and I don’t pay for mobile data, and you quickly realize it isn’t for you.

I dumped Spotify and switched to Apple Music. I liked it more right off the bat; there are a lot less distractions because the app is based on Apple’s built-in music player. The music sounds a lot better because of Apple’s AAC format, and there were some albums which Apple Music had that Spotify didn’t. 

The Dolby Atmos mixes of albums like The Dark Side of The Moon absolutely blew me away and the interface overall seemed more intuitive. 

For a time, I was happier, but I knew that this couldn’t last forever. 

###LISTENING HABITS – DISCOVERY & RENAISSANCE

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_7122_04.webp"><span><i>An Der Schonen, Blauen Donau</i>—one of my all-time favourite classical releases and one of the first Compact Discs to be released in the early 1980's. Note the smooth top and bottoms of the Jewel Case. This release sounds like liquid gold!</span></div>

Seven years ago, around the same time I started using Spotify’s free tier, I started a CD collection. It was something I always wanted having grown up with them; it was just how I always imagined my life to be. The buildup has always been slow—I rarely ever buy albums new, preferring to frequent Thrift Stores and get them for a dollar or two each. 

After around a year or two of collecting, I was still discovering some music on Spotify, but I would mostly just stream the digital versions of the albums I had physically. It doesn’t make sense, but that’s just how I listen to music. I have a hard time jumping into a new album because that always feels like a big event, and in many ways, it is. 

Discovery is a magical, cathartic phase which I treasure because you can only ever listen to something once. I’ll stick with an album and listen to it over and over again until the dopamine stops, and by then, I’ll have learned to truly appreciate every track. Sometimes its weeks, and sometimes it doesn’t leave my rotation for months. 

After that honeymoon discovery phase pitters out, I’ll listen to it on occasion until something clicks and it goes through what I call a “Renaissance.” I’ve found that no matter what album it is, if I enjoyed it tremendously once, it will always have a renaissance eventually where it returns to the top of my rotation. I don’t skip tracks—I just listen to the whole thing front to back how the artist intended it. Simply by listening, I’ve come to appreciate every album in my collection as it grows steadily. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_15749_11.webp"><span>The Rush and Phil Collins section of my collection.</span></div>

I never found the algorithm on Spotify or Apple Music to be of much help recommending music. Sometimes when I go to a thrift store, I’ll see an album I think I’ll like and I buy it; I’ve got a pretty good feel for that. Sometimes I’ll buy an album simply because I’ve heard people or the collective conscience speak highly of it. People will recommend albums and artists to me and I’ll seek copies of them out in the world to enjoy rather than just seeking it out on the net. 

Discovery, for me, has been slow and organic—just the way I like it. I wouldn’t rush into a relationship with a woman, so why would I rush into a relationship with an album? Sure there’s the anticipation, excitement and pleasure of getting to know her, but none of that can be rushed, nor should it be treated as disposable. 

It means that albums have a natural entry into my life; it’s something I could never reconcile when I would try and discover new albums on a streaming service. How do you decide when to move on from your current phase and discover something new? Albums never enter your life when streaming, they’re forced down your throat or you have to seek them out. 

There are many ways to listen to music and discover it, and I’ve found that this way works best for me and how I consume media. Whatever it is—and it’s all in my head—something isn’t compatible with the way streaming services are set up. There’s no guy at the record store to enthuse about something, nor is there limited stock—you’re just flooded by too much choice.

###LISTENING TO MUSIC – BUILDING A LIBRARY

<div class="blog-image blog-image-med blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_26482_10.webp"><span>My first-generation iPod Touch which I used daily throughout the 2010's and later in the early 2020's before I bought the endgame iPod Classic.</span></div>

As time went on, I was increasingly frustrated by the caveats of streaming. At home, I would happily listen to music on my ever-growing Hi-Fi stack—it started with Sony bookshelf stereos and turned into a real audio system when I found a Sony 5-Disc CD Changer at a thrift store for cheap. 

I’ve got a magnificent stack of all thrifted Sony machines sitting in my room with which I consume media—the aforementioned 5-Disc changer, a tape deck, a receiver/amplifier, a DVD player and a VCR. 

My Sony CDP-E315 doesn’t need the internet and there’s no irritating interface which gets constantly changed and updated. There are no distractions when plucking an album off the shelf and queuing it up. Those who listen to Vinyl will understand this aspect. 

As my collection grew in size and hit around 400 albums, there was really no need to stream anymore. I decided that it was silly to pay an ever-increasing amount per month when I could spend that on a couple of used CDs I own perpetually and I enjoy more. I decided that anything I didn’t own I wouldn’t listen to since there’s plenty to enjoy already—I can just wait until I find copies of it if I want it. 

The problem was how I would listen to music on the go. As a trial run, I dug out my old first-generation iPod Touch and loaded it with 320kb/s AAC files—compressed copies of my CDs which were higher quality than Spotify and roughly the same quality as Apple Music. 

I never streamed again after that. 

###A COMPLETE LISTENING SETUP

It quickly became apparent as my collection grew that the 8GB iPod Touch wasn’t going to cut it forever. I bought myself a 7th Generation iPod Classic off eBay and modded it to have a bigger battery and 512GB of MicroSD flash storage. I enjoy taking things apart and finagling with them so it was pretty easy with the help of online guides—much easier than fixing old Hi-Fi equipment. 

I’ve been using that iPod now for three years whenever I’m on the go, and I listen to my CDs when I’m at home. I’ve since re-ripped my entire collection as lossless FLAC files so I’ve got full CD-quality music all the time at my fingertips. I find it very convenient and I like it a lot; there’s just no fuss with it most of the time. 

The iPod fits in the small coin pocket of my shorts or jeans almost perfectly, I don’t have to deal with the stupidity of Bluetooth earbuds and there are no distractions or limitations—just music. I’ve never run out of battery and I never charge the iPod, either; the charge trickles when I sync it every couple of days and it seemingly lasts perpetually. 

At around 730 albums, you might think that I’m drowning in too much choice like I said earlier with streaming. This is far from the case. Because I built up this library slowly and deliberately, it doesn’t feel nearly as fast as the number sounds. I know almost every one of those albums intimately track-by-track because I’ve curated it so carefully. 

I have complete control over which releases and masters of albums I have in my collection; again, something not many people think of, but it’s something which became important to me when realizing the sheer difference in quality between original masters and so-called “remasters”. That’s a story for another day, though. 

I enjoy my routines of scroll-wheeling through my library on the iPod or even just staring at my shelves filled with the albums which make me who I am today. I love reading through the booklets or admiring the album art. I love handling the discs and coming to know all the eras of moulds and jewel cases. 

I’ve also calculated that I’ve actually spent less money over the past seven years buying CDs than I have/would have spent streaming—a crossover point which is becoming increasingly distant with the ever-rising costs of streaming services.

###THE CAVEATS

<div class="blog-image blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_8814_06.webp"><span>The innards of my iPod—the expanded battery and headphone jack/hold switch.</span></div>

Like I said earlier, I don’t think that this is for everyone. I don’t think that everyone should go back to listening on CDs, Vinyl and MP3 players as many people online seem to be evangelizing. 

I’m a certain kind of persnickety person and listener. It takes a certain kind of persnickety to fixate on individual masters of albums, repair broken stereo equipment and maintain a massive library. 

Personally, I don’t like Vinyl as a practical format because of the amount of care you have to exercise over records with dusting, tone-arms, needles and sleeves. It just feels clunky and overwrought. I enjoy the crisp perfection of a CD, their easily replaceable jewel cases and the fact that it works so well as a versatile format in this digital age. 

It is, admittedly, sometimes very inconvenient to listen to music the way I do. Unless I go search it up on YouTube, I don’t listen to songs or albums when they first come out. Unless it’s a band I really like, I’m not going to show up at a record store day one and spend on a full-priced album. 

I also recognize that there is a lot of music which now only exists digitally. This isn’t usually a problem for me since I’m not into a lot of the super modern rap-based, hip-hop-based or electronic-based music, but I know that I’m missing out some really kick-ass stuff I’d otherwise like. 

The only way to rationalize this is that music has been around for so long that you don’t really need to fixate on everything that’s new since there’s so much which already exists. I don’t really read books as soon as they come out because there’s already so many books already published which I want to read and are more readily available. A good amount of the time, if an album is popular enough, I’ll grab it on CD as soon as it begins circulating at my public library. The library is another great way I discover music because it too is a collection which has been curated by groups of people. 

<div class="blog-image blog-image blog-image-left"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_8844_07.webp"><span>A macro shot of the headphone jack on my iPod.</span></div>

Stuff breaks, too. I’ve had two bookshelf stereos give out on me in the three years I was using them since their CD mechanisms weren’t designed for the amount of use I put them through. My CD changer has held up much better since it’s originally more expensive, proper hi-fi gear, but I know that it too will eventually run into issues. 

I bought my tape deck, VCR and DVD player all broken at heavy discounts, and I fixed them after flipping through old service manuals I found online. I still have to regularly service my tape deck so that it runs smoothly; these things aren’t for everyone, though it’s not a problem you’d really run into if you bought things brand new. 

The same goes for the iPod. The reality is that it’s an almost twenty year old device—well-past a full drivers license, legal drinking age and the minimum requirement to set up a retirement savings plan.

The headphone jack, especially when you carry it around in your pants around the belt all day, does get worn down over time. I’ve had to replace it twice, which isn’t really bad in two years considering the amount of use I get out of it, and that the part is less than $10. Still, it’s something to consider. 

The thing I think would be the most inconvenient for people is the sourcing of the music files, library management and the impracticalities of syncing. I only have to sync my iPod every couple of days to periodically update my playlists, but there are many people who live off playlists. If you want your podcasts updated every day, you’ve got to remember to plug in the iPod and sync it. It’ll become part of your routine, but it is a deliberate consideration which has to be made. 

The music doesn’t come easily from the void like it does in streaming. You won’t have the convenience of searching up that one song someone recommended—if you really want it, you’ve got to find a source, download it, import it and then sync it. 

I like being very persnickety with my iTunes library. I’ve got every single album ripped in lossless ALAC format with the album art scanned at 1500x1500 pixels. Almost all the metadata fields are filled out, and I’ve even adjusted the Sort Order so that albums appear in proper chronological order. It’s not something that everyone is going to want to do—I just happen to enjoy such tasks.

It’s a very different way of listening to music than streaming. In the way that journalists called my generation “Internet Natives”, there are people who are native to the album and physical media era, and those who are native to this current streaming era. I imagine it would be difficult for someone to adjust to an objectively more inconvenient and impractical way of listening to music if they’ve grown up with something convenient—and even if you start, it’s difficult for many to keep it going and stick with it. 

###WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

<div class="blog-image blog-image blog-image-right"><img src="/blog/resources/2026-03-06_DSC_12818_08.webp"><span>A now outdated photo of my Hi-Fi stack—this is before I got the new, matching, Sony VCR.</span></div>

It’s easy for me to go all-in on physical media because that’s how I’m built, mentally; it’s the kind of music-listener I am, and it works for the kinds of music that I’m into. 
At the end of the day—and this is the most important part—I listen to music on the Compact Disc and my iPod Classic not because it saves money, not because it’s practical or because I feel it’s better than streaming. 

It’s for the **love of the game**—that’s the only reason why you should do *anything*. 

I do this because my younger self would’ve thought it was absolutely wicked that I’ve got shelves of well-known and random bands from every era spanning many genres. I love  music more than probably anything else in this world, and it’s my way of immersing myself in it as deeply as I can.
