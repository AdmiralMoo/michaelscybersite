A couple of weeks ago, I was browsing an online auction site in search of more camera gear when I came across the kind of listing I was searching for. I’ve been looking for film cameras, camcorders and digital cameras from the late 1990s and early 2000s to take around and shoot—stuff that offers a fun and different shooting experience than what I have now. The camera I spotted was a curious silver rectangle with many bevels, fillets and chamfers—a fine example of wacky 2000s design. 

It was the FinePix 4800z: a compact point-and-shoot released in 2001 and was shaped by legendary designer Ferdinand Alexander Porsche—the same guy who designed the classic Porsche 911! As I read more about the camera, I realised that it was not only a charming-looking thing, but also a very technically impressive package that could produce beautiful images even today. 

Needless to say, I was fascinated, and I won the auction rather easily.

### REVIVAL

The Saturday I picked up the camera was absolutely packed. I rode my bike north and then just east of Ottawa’s downtown core to the pickup site: a small apartment in a sleepy suburb. I climbed two sets of carpeted, uneven stairs and traveled to the end of a dingey blue hallway to the apartment, where I was greeted by an attendant who had all the pickups ready. 

The camera was almost complete in box, featuring the device itself and all the accompanying paperwork. It didn’t, however, have any cables or the dock that came with it. There was a battery in the camera, but I had no way to know whether or not it worked. 

Alas, I had the camera and I’d figure it out from there. I continued on my adventure and rode to Bank St. where I perused The Great Glebe Garage sale, which you can read about in my music collection blog post. 

When I got home, I did some research about the power connector and found a universal power supply I could get from a store not too far away. I hurried out again to complete a Facebook Marketplace transaction and I stopped by the store on my way back. 

I matched the 5V DC in connector and plugged it in—it powered on! I set the date, time and was greeted by a live view, but I couldn’t take any photos—the little porthole LCD simply said “ERR”, and the lens wouldn’t deploy or retract when I tried to power cycle it. 

I flipped the switch into playback mode and found photos of the previous owner—it seems the camera was last used in 2014 by someone who owned goats. 

I set about searching for service manuals and clues as to what was going on with the camera, and I took it apart. Eight small Phillips screws held the two halves of sturdy, silver plastic housing together. Nothing looked out of place, but the forum I searched said that one of the ribbon cables—one connecting the mainboard to the automatic lens cover—had a weak connection. I reseated the ribbon cable, put it all back together and plugged it in again. 

This time, it worked! I was able to take photos and record them to the card successfully. The only obstacle now was that it seemed the battery was completely dead—it wouldn’t charge when it was plugged in and supplied nothing to the camera. 

I ordered an NP-80 replacement battery off Amazon, but cancelled the order the next day because I’d found a battery warehouse site that sold it cheaper and would arrive faster. Despite me having cancelled the first order—in fact, I never paid for it in the first place—I received both batteries in the mail. I won’t complain about that!

At around the same time the first battery came in the mail, I also got a USB-C SmartMedia card reader; I was planning on getting one anyways because my Samsung SCD77 uses the same format. 

Finally, I was fully equipped and ready to take it out. My first excursion was to the climbing gym after I’d finished work.

The FinePix 4800z, as I previously said, was designed by F.A. Porsche—the designer of the Porsche 911 and grandson of Ferdinand Porsche, the founder of the eponymous company. That, I suppose, would account for the camera’s unconventional layout and interface. 

The 4800z is held vertically in the right hand as if you were holding a smartphone. It has an optical viewfinder on the top left side which is linked to the zoom mechanism of the lens so that you have an approximate preview even when taking full advantage of the full-frame equivalent 36–108mm zoom. One’s right thumb rests in a depression on the right side of the camera or on the directional control pad and the shutter is triggered by the index finger. 

I find it very comfortable to shoot with—it’s much thicker than a smartphone and is designed in such a way which makes this style of shooting feel like second nature. If you were to design a grip for a smartphone, I believe it’d be shaped much like this camera. 

Part of the reason why I bought this camera was because it has that optical viewfinder—my biggest gripe with my Canon PowerShot A2200 HD is that it only has live view. After “upgrading” from the Nikon D40 to the Nikon Z6, I find myself missing the OVF experience, so shooting with this camera against my face feels fantastic: it feels like a real camera. 

Holding the power button for a fraction of a second will power the device on and a couple things happen at once. The circular lens cover bearing the F.A. Porsche name slides down inside of the device and the lens extends. 

The back of the camera features two screens: a full colour LCD display which shows the menus, live view and image playback as well as a circular monochrome LCD which shows messages and controls. When in shooting mode, this porthole glows orange; in playback mode, it glows green. Mode is set by a switch attached to the power button. 

When the camera starts, the porthole greets you with a “Hello!” and says “See you” when the camera turns off. It’s quite pleasant; technology isn’t really friendly like this anymore. 

It takes around four and a half seconds until the camera is ready to shoot, but because the live view display isn’t on all the time, you can comfortably walk around with it turned on and ready to shoot as you go about your business. 

Around the porthole are four directional buttons; their purpose changes depending on the mode or screen and the porthole LCD shows what their current function is. In shooting mode, the top button zooms in, the bottom zooms out, the left button toggles Macro focusing and the right toggles flash settings. The flash is deployed by a dedicated “OPEN” button—though my flash is unfortunately missing a spring so it doesn’t pop out on its own. To the left of the porthole is a “BACK” and “MENU/OK” button as well as a “DISP” button to toggle the larger colour screen. 

In the top right corner of the camera there’s a knurled mode dial which feels good to use; the camera features a continuous shooting mode, a manual mode, an automatic mode, a scene mode for night and portrait shooting, a video mode and a voice memo mode.

I tend to shoot in the Manual mode, though it’s not as manual as you’d think it is. Really, it just opens up the ability to adjust the ISO and exposure compensation—but I’ll take as much control as I can get. I prefer my photos to be underexposed, generally, and almost every camera I’ve ever used blows out the sky and highlights more than I’d like. 

It didn’t take long to get used to the odd vertical form factor of the camera, nor did it take long to fall in love with the optical viewfinder. I think it’s a joy to shoot with despite the unconventional design which I initially thought was ridiculous. Upon first impressions, it’s an unsightly thing, but it makes sense once you use it—the exact same way I feel about the “nipple” found on ThinkPads. 

I walked down the street with the camera and took photos of things that intrigued me as I normally would. I found it easy and satisfying to shoot with; although there’s no display in the viewfinder for exposure information, you can see what shutter speed and aperture the camera decided on by switching on the live view screen. I’ve found this is helpful when shooting in low light so you know how hard to have to try to be still—ha!

That said, though, the optical viewfinder and form factor makes it very easy to stabilize in low light. Crank the ISO up to the maximum of 400 and hold it up to your face like you would any camera—if you can shoot a film camera at ¼, you can definitely shoot this one. There’s no image stabilization of any kind, and although that makes it a little difficult to handle at times, I think it makes it more fun to shoot with. I’ve always thought the ability to stabilize a camera at longer shutter speeds is part of the fun and skill of photography. 

I shot with it there and back, and I was very happy with the results. 

### THE PHOTOS – FUJIFILM MAGIC

The 4800z boasts a 1/17-inch Super CCD sensor which produces stunning 2400x1800 JPEGs. File size sits at around 1.7MB per image and has impressive dynamic range and beautiful colour right off the camera. Normally, you’d think that JPEGs can’t yield much flexibility in software such as Lightroom, but these files have a lot of play because Fujifilm was careful with how they encoded their image data. 

That’s just one of the many small things this camera does really well. Fujifilm—obviously—is known for their rich history in developing beloved colour films. Early on in the digital camera era, they had a huge leg up on their competitors thanks to their mature in-house colour science. This advantage shows instantly with the 4800z’s photos coming out pleasantly saturated and lively. It’s not overdone—it looks natural and light falloff feels like you’re looking at a real photo and not a smartphone image—but it feels a little sweeter, maybe more dream-like, than most other digital camera photos. 

Greens are verdant and lush as you’d expect from a Fujifilm camera, and the blue of the sky tends to shift towards purple in that pleasant, dreamy way I just love. Oranges and reds pop out, as do bright pinks. I just love the way photos come out on this thing.

The camera tends to try and shoot wide open at f/2.8 as often as possible which I absolutely love. This introduces a softness around the edges of the photos with a fair bit of chromatic aberration. If you’re shooting with this camera, it’s not because you want a clinical, sterile CMOS-style image—you want life and character; you want it to look like a photo. I shoot my Nikon Series E lenses wide open at f/2.8 because I love this exact look, so it was just great to find this camera does the same thing. 

All the hype you see online about the SuperCCD sensor is definitely real. These images are detailed and good-looking; they’ve got Fujifilm magic sprinkled all over along with optical artifacts from an imperfect lens. 

Bright lights glow with a very subtle and natural bloom on this sensor; it’s quite a pleasing effect for golden hour and sunny shots. Overall, I would say that this camera does best in moody lighting or bright, sunny days where the colours have a chance to pop—though I suppose all photos are pretty blah on a flat, white, cloudy day. 

When you’ve got a quicker shutter speed or good stabilization, this thing is sharp as a tack. The zoom is pretty good, too—I mentioned earlier that it has a 35–106-equivalent lens, but there’s also a digital zoom if you don’t have the camera set to its maximum image quality level.

Thankfully, the previous owner of the camera was absolutely ballin’ and had a 128mb SmartMedia card—a rare and expensive luxury which provides roughly 76 images: a little over two rolls of film. You can zoom in a little on the images and things look sharp all the way down, quite unlike a modern phone photo with its empty, mushy, gross pixels and filters.

I’m someone who grew up with early 2000’s Nikon colour science so the colours coming out of this camera are very appealing to me. It doesn’t really offer the same kind of crunchy, honestly crappy “digicam” look many are looking for these days when searching for old cameras—it’s genuinely good for what it was back then and holds up today. 

This, along with all the other quirks and considerations, means this isn’t the old point and shoot camera for everybody, but it makes it insanely appealing for someone like me.

### TRIVIAL QUIRKS & CAVEATS

It was a bit of a hassle getting this camera to work, but that’s not the kind of thing I shy away from. I enjoy taking things apart and fixing them, and while it took a while to get everything together, I’m thoroughly enjoying taking this camera around to go shooting. 

I’m surprised that someone woke up in the past five years and said “today, I’m going to make a USB-C SmartMedia card reader.” But hey, it happened, and it works—I’m keeping it in my laptop pouch since it also has an SD card slot. 

SmartMedia is a very antiquated, finnicky format which hails from the time of the floppy disk. The cards are shaped like miniature floppy disks with the diagonal cutout and there even existed SmartMedia to floppy disk adapters—in fact, the 4800z was supposed to come with one in the box. It’s not a practical or cheap format to acquire these days, with just 8MB cards setting you back $30, and 128MB cards being resold for up to $250. Honestly, at this point, I think the cheapest way to get more SmartMedia cards is to buy up cheap cameras and electronics that used them and salvage whatever comes with it. 

They don’t have onboard controllers like SD Cards do, so if you eject them while the device is turned on, the whole thing is going to get corrupted. You’ve got to be very careful with these thin, delicate cards. 

I wrote a little bit about stabilization earlier; I really wasn’t kidding—you’ll end up with lots of blurry photos if you don’t hold this camera properly. If you’re a Sony shooter and you hold the thing out in front of you instead of using the viewfinder, you’ll find it hard to get sharp images. 

Maybe it’s just my unit, but when you power the camera on, it always starts ever so slightly zoomed in; you might not even notice it, and it’s only worth maybe 5mm of focal length, but you have to hold the down arrow to get that extra drop of wide angle goodness if you really want it. 

When you take the batteries out, it loses almost all of the settings. There’s no onboard battery or thing-that-holds-a-charge to preserve the time, exposure compensation and image quality while you swap out batteries so you’ll have to either keep the thing charged or reset your settings every time. 

The colour LCD display definitely isn’t colour accurate and the viewing angles are really bad. This kind of goes without saying when dealing with older electronics and it doesn’t bother me, but it’ll sometimes have you questioning whether or not your images are exposed correctly. You’ve just got to get used to the camera and trust that you’ve got it right; I think the worst thing you can do when you’re taking photos is get too hung up on what’s coming out of the screen on the back, and I tend not to use image replay on any of my cameras so that I stay in the moment and just enjoy shooting. 

I don’t really have anything negative to say about this camera, honestly. Setting the time, date and image quality is a bit of a bother every time I swap batteries or lose a charge, but even that is just something you have to deal with when using various electronics. 

The overall design of this camera is charming, comfortable, functional, fun to use, and I love the images that it spits out. The interface is elegant, simple and harkens back to a much more primitive time when computers and electronics weren’t trying to be things they’re not. 

Already in just a week of it being fully functional, I’ve taken five hundred photos with it. I didn’t expect to love this camera as much as I do, and I can’t wait to take it on many more adventures.
